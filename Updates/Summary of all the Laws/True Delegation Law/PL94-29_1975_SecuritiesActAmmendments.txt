Conference Report
Congress May 22 gave final approval to legislation revising federal securities
regulatory laws to encourage development of a national system for buying
and selling stocks.
The culmination of a four-year review by House and Senate subcommittees,
the measure (S 249—PL 94 - 29 ) cleared the way for integration of
existing securities markets into a more open and competitive system.
S 249 did not spell out the exact shape of that system—which was expected
to use computers and electronic communications to link existing separate
securities markets—but it removed federal regulations and industry practices
that restricted investors' access to the nation's 13 stock exchanges and over-
the-counter market. While preserving the self-regulatory framework
established under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, the measure
significantly enlarged the Securities and Exchange Commission's (SEC)
oversight powers over the stock exchanges and the National Association of
Securities Dealers (NASD), which supervises the over-the-counter market
outside the exchanges.
Final action came when the House by voice vote adopted a conference report
that generally followed the Senate version of the bill. The Senate had adopted
the conference report by voice vote on May 20.
New York Stock Exchange Chairman James J. Needham termed S 249 “the
most substantial and significant reform of the federal securities laws in more
than 40 years.” The final version, he added, met “many of the concerns of the
securities industry” about various proposals that Congress had considered.
In the case of the New York exchange (NYSE), at least, the House-Senate
conferees met those objections by dropping House provisions that would have
abolished stock exchange seats and revoked a NYSE rule that forces its
members to do business only on the exchange floor.
The bill made other changes in the way stock exchanges operate, however,
notably by eventually prohibiting exchange members to buy or sell stocks for
themselves or for an affiliated company and by upholding the SEC's order
that abolished fixed brokerage commissions on May 1, 1975.
Other major provisions would extend SEC regulation to firms that process
securities transaction paperwork and to banks that underwrite and trade state
and local government bonds. The bill also required banks, insurance
companies and other large-scale institutional investors to disclose their
holdings of various securities to the SEC.
The legislation reflected the conclusions reached by separate securities
industry studies completed in 1972 by the House Interstate and Foreign
Commerce Subcommittee on Commerce and Finance chaired by Rep. John E.
Moss (D Calif.), and in 1973 by the Senate Banking, Housing and Urban
Affairs Subcommittee on Securities chaired by Sen. Harrison A. Williams Jr. (D
N.J.).

Provisions
As signed into law June 4, major provisions of S 249:
Directed the SEC to encourage development of a national system for the
efficient buying and selling of securities using communications and data
processing facilities.
Gave the SEC power to regulate securities information processing firms that
collect and publish stock quotations and record transactions.
Directed the SEC to review within 90 days stock exchange rules that limit a
member's ability to transact business in another market. If it found such a rule
inconsistent with industry competition, the SEC could start proceedings to
revoke the rule within another 90 days.
Gave the SEC “fail-safe powers” to prohibit “third market” trading on the over-
the-counter market in securities that were listed on a stock exchange. That
authority could be used, however, only to prevent serious market disruptions
resulting from the switch to competitively negotiated brokerage commissions
charged by exchange members.
Established a 15-member advisory board to help oversee development of a
national market system.
Required a stock exchange to admit any qualified broker or dealer but allowed an exchange to limit its total membership
to a level no lower than its membership on May 1, 1975.
Required prior SEC approval of significant rule changes proposed by a self-regulatory body.
Extended federal securities regulation to securities firms and banks that underwrite and trade state and local
government bonds.
Established a Municipal Securities Rulemaking Board to develop rules for trading in municipal securities.
Gave the SEC authority to regulate firms that process securities transaction paperwork. Federal bank regulatory
agencies would retain primary responsibility, however, for supervising securities processing functions performed by
banks.
Authorized stepped-up SEC efforts to investigate missing, lost, counterfeit or stolen securities.
Directed the SEC to establish by Sept. 1, 1975, minimum capital requirements for all brokers and dealers.
Prohibited a stock exchange member to buy and sell securities for his own account or for an affiliated financial
institution or other related business. The provision would bar banks, insurance companies and other financial institutions
from buying stock exchange seats for an affiliated firm to save brokerage costs; it also would keep brokers from
branching out into money management services for their clients.
Prohibited stock exchanges to fix the commission rates that members charge public customers, thus upholding an SEC
ruling effective May 1, 1975. In accordance with that ruling, commissions could still be fixed until  May 1, 1976, on floor
transactions handled by a member on behalf of another member.
Allowed the SEC to reimpose fixed commissions by rule before Nov. 1, 1976. After that date, the SEC could restore
fixed commissions only after formal proceedings.
Required institutional investment managers to make periodic disclosures of their stock holdings and transactions.
Protected investment managers against possible charges of violating fiduciary obligations simply because they paid
brokerage commissions that were higher than the lowest available fees under a competitive rate system.
Authorized appropriations of $51-million in fiscal 1976 and $55-million in fiscal 1977 for SEC operations.

incorporated provisions drawn from five separate securities bills considered during the 93rd Congress. The Senate
passed four of those measures in 1973-74, but the House Interstate and Foreign Commerce Committee held them

With some significant changes—including the addition of provisions requiring open stock exchange membership—
the committee broadened SEC oversight powers and directed the commission to move toward a central market
system. The Senate had approved similar legislation by passing S 2519 by voice vote on May 28, 1974. That
measure also provided standby SEC authority to forbid third-market trading. (Action, 1974 Almanac p. 193)
Updating another bill (S 470) passed by the Senate in 1973, the committee recommended provisions to set
standards for any return to fixed rates and to phase in a prohibition on self-dealing. S 470 had linked the two issues,
permitting institutional membership on stock exchanges but forbidding them to buy and sell securities for their own
accounts once commissions were competitive. (1973 Almanac p. 272)
S 249 also included provisions from:
S 2058, passed by the Senate in 1973, to require regulation of the processing of securities transactions and remove
impediments to a national processing system. (1973 Almanac p. 275)
S 2474, passed by the Senate on Sept. 16, 1974, to impose federal regulation of state and local bond trading. (1974
Almanac p. 210)
S 2234, considered by the committee in 1974, to require public disclosure of stock investments by institutional investors
such as banks, insurance companies and pension funds.
Senate Floor Action
The Senate passed S 249 by voice vote April 17. Before passage, the Senate adopted two floor amendments
endorsed by Harrison A. Williams Jr. (D N.J.), chairman of the subcommittee that drafted the bill.
Negotiated Fees
Although no specific mandates were included, the Senate bill indirectly confirmed the SEC's Jan. 23 ruling that
required stock brokers to negotiate the fees that they charge their public customers for transactions executed on the
exchanges after April 30. That would end the fixed commissions dictated by the stock exchanges since the New
York Stock Exchange was founded in 1792.
Although the Williams subcommittee concluded that the SEC already had adequate authority to require negotiated
commissions, its bill provided standards for determining reasonable brokerage fees if the commission found that
market disruptions required a return to fixed commissions.
Third Market
To meet objections to negotiated commissions raised by the New York Stock Exchange and industry groups, S 249
gave the SEC standby authority to bar “third market” trading on the over-the-counter market in stocks that were
listed on exchanges.
By using that authority, the SEC could remedy any market disruptions that industry officials warned would result if
the end of fixed commissions prompted exchange members to give up their seats. That power could be used only
until April 30, 1978—with provision for one-year extensions—or until a national market system was created.
Self Dealing
Resolving an industry controversy over stock exchange membership by brokers affiliated with financial institutions
that make substantial securities investments, the bill would prohibit an exchange member from buying or selling
securities for its own account or for an affiliated firm.
Phased in over two years, the provision would end potential conflicts of interest posed by the trend toward
combining stock brokerage with management of investment accounts for customers—both by brokers branching out
into money management and by financial institutions that sought stock exchange seats for affiliated firms to cut
investment costs. It overturned a 1974 SEC ruling that required an exchange member to do 80 per cent of its
business with non-affiliated customers.
Other Provisions
Other major provisions of S 249 would:
Open stock exchange membership to all qualified brokers and dealers, thus abolishing the concept of a limited number
of stock exchange seats that must be bought to gain membership.
Direct the SEC to take steps to develop an integrated national market system.
Extend the SEC authority to regulate firms that transmit stock prices and quotation information, firms that record and
process securities transactions, and brokers who do all their business on exchange floors.

Floor Amendments
By voice votes, the Senate adopted floor amendments to S 249:
By Henry M. Jackson (D Wash.) to require securities firms to determine whether stocks they handle have been reported
as stolen or counterfeit and to report any counterfeit, lost or stolen securities to the SEC.
By Williams to allow the securities industry to set up a self-regulatory National Market System Council to oversee
development of a national system under SEC supervision. The amendment also made clear that state and local
governments themselves would not be subject to regulatory disclosure requirements and exempted from SEC regulation
notes secured by mortgage liens and sold by banking institutions to generate housing market funds.
House Committee Action
The House Interstate and Foreign Commerce Committee reported its version of the bill April 7 (HR 4111—H Rept 
94 -123). The committee made only a few revisions in 1974 legislation that died in the House Rules Committee.
(1974 Almanac p. 193)
Like the committee's 1974 measure (HR 5050), HR 4111 would eliminate barriers to competition, enlarge the
Securities and Exchange Commission's (SEC) regulatory powers and encourage development of a nationwide
securities market system linked by computers and electronic communications.
The committee incorporated most significant provisions from HR 5050 in its 1975 measure, including controversial
proposals to end fixed commission rates, open stock exchange membership to all qualified brokers and dealers, bar
self-dealing by members for their own or affiliated accounts and overturn a New York Stock Exchange rule against
third-market trading by its members.
Fixed Commissions
The committee once again recommended that Congress give legislative authority to the SEC's ruling that would
abolish fixed stock exchange commission rates as of May 1, 1975. Modifying the measure to conform with a revision
in the SEC's stance, the committee approved provisions to allow the exchange to continue fixing floor brokerage
commissions until May 1, 1976.
In formally adopting in January its rule requiring that stock commission fees be open to negotiation, the SEC allowed
a one-year exemption for floor brokerage fees to give the industry more time to prepare for that adjustment. Floor
brokerage commissions are fees that are paid by one stock broker to another broker who is an exchange member
for executing a transaction on the stock exchange floor.
As did the committee's 1974 legislative proposal, HR 4111 gave the SEC authority to allow the exchanges to keep
fixed rates in effect on transactions of $300,000 or less until Oct. 1, 1976, to avoid market disruptions. Any
extension beyond Oct. 1, 1976, would require a more formal SEC determination that fixed rates were necessary to
the industry's health.
Major Revisions
In changes from the 1974 measure, the committee approved provisions that:
Directed the SEC to appoint a 15-member board to recommend steps toward implementing a fully integrated national
market system.
Authorized SEC appropriations for fiscal 1976–78 but dropped a separate fiscal 1979 authorization.
Clarified the conditions under which an investment adviser to a mutual fund can sell his business for a profit.
The committee rewrote provisions designed to make clear that bank trust departments and other institutional money
managers would not legally violate fiduciary standards merely because they paid higher commissions on stock
transactions than the lowest rates available after fixed commissions were abolished.
Bowing to Ford administration opposition, the committee also dropped provisions in the 1974 bill that would have
required SEC commissioners to leave office within 60 days after their terms expired and prohibited executive branch
agencies from requiring the SEC to clear testimony or budget requests before submission to Congress.

The House April 24 passed HR 4111 by a 376-13 vote. (Vote 109, p. 38-H)
Before passage, the House adopted three amendments by voice votes.
The first, offered by Harley O. Staggers (D W. Va.), would require institutional investors to make public disclosure of
their security holdings and transactions each year.
John E. Moss (D Calif.), who had been chairman of the subcommittee that drafted the bill in the 93rd Congress,
offered a three-part amendment dealing with the 15-member board set up under HR 4111. His amendment would
require that the board members be from different geographic areas and would allow anyone to seek judicial review
of the SEC's appointments to the board. It also prohibited the board from using the SEC's subpoena powers in
order to get documents.
A third amendment, offered by Bob Eckhardt (D Texas), would permit the SEC to separate itself from other
defendants in civil suits.
After passing HR 4111, the House substituted its text for the language of S 249 and passed S 249 by voice vote.
Conference Report
House and Senate conferees reached agreement on S 249 on May 14, missing by two weeks the May 1 target for
final congressional action that House leaders had hoped to meet. The SEC's rule outlawing fixed commission rates
went into effect on May 1. The conference report on the bill (H Rept 94-229) was filed May 19.
Third Market
Final agreement was delayed while conferees worked out differences in the way the House and Senate versions
dealt with the New York Stock Exchange's regulation (Rule 394) that restricted members' dealing in stocks on other
markets.
The House version (HR 4111) required that any such exchange rule that limited or put conditions on a member's
ability to do business in other markets be eliminated by Sept. 1, 1975, unless the SEC specifically sanctioned its
continuation. The Senate bill, however, merely directed the SEC to review all exchange rules and force elimination
of those that restrained competition.
Elimination of Rule 394 could have been especially damaging to NYSE “specialists”—members working on the
exchange floor who buy and sell stocks only for their own accounts or as agents for other members. If other
members were able to seek better terms in other securities markets, the specialists stood to lose business.
As a compromise, the conferees agreed on provisions directing the SEC to review such rules within 90 days. If the
commission found the rules violated competitive standards spelled out by the bill, it could start proceedings to
eliminate or modify the exchange regulations within another 90 days.
Exchange Seats
Following the thrust of the Senate version, the conferees approved provisions that allowed stock exchanges to
continue to limit the number of members holding seats that allowed brokers to do business on their floors.
As passed by the House, HR 4111 would have required that stock exchange membership be open to all brokers
and dealers who met capital and competency requirements. Effective in 1978, those House provisions would have
abolished the concept of stock exchange seats that must be purchased from an existing member to gain access to
the trading floor.
The Senate bill, while also directing exchanges to admit any qualified member, still acknowledged an exchange's
right to limit the physical number of members who could reasonably use its facilities.
In the conference agreement, House conferees accepted provisions that allowed an exchange to limit its
membership so long as the number was not reduced below its May 1, 1975, level. The compromise gave the SEC
express power to force an exchange to enlarge its membership.
Other Provisions
In other conference decisions, conferees:
Accepted House provisions creating an advisory board to help the SEC develop a national market system. The Senate
version would have established a self-regulatory industry council to supervise development of that system.
Generally followed the thrust of the Senate bill by giving existing federal bank regulatory agencies prime regulatory
responsibility for the banks' role in processing securities transactions. The House version gave greater direct power over
such bank activities to the SEC.
