Credit-Card Disclosure
An article from CQ Almanac 1988
The House Oct. 20 cleared a bill (HR 515 — PL 100 - 583) that required issurers of credit and charge cards to
show clearly on direct-mail solicitations information such as the annual percentage interest rate, annual fees and
other costs associated with the use of the cards. Similar disclosure was required for telephone solicitations and
magazine advertising.
States were prohibited from requiring additional disclosure by credit-card issuers. However, the Federal Reserve
Board could require additional items to be disclosed.
Sponsors said the bill was aimed at bulk-mail credit-card solicitations that tempted potential customers while not
disclosing all applicable fees and costs. The House and Senate both had approved versions of the bill in 1987.
(1987 Almanac p. 636)
President Reagan signed the bill into law Nov. 3.
Major Provisions
As cleared, HR 515:
Required all card issuers to disclose in writing annual percentage interest rates; annual fees; the grace period before
interest began to accrue; the method of computing card balances subject to interest charges; minimum finance charges;
and fees for cash advances, late payments and transactions that exceeded credit limits.
Telephone and magazine solicitations, and so-called “take-one” credit-card applications, had somewhat different
disclosure requirements from direct-mail and face-to-face solicitations. All required information had to be disclosed if
any details were provided. Otherwise, telephone and similar solicitations had to disclose only that costs would be
incurred through use of credit cards, and applicants would be given telephone numbers to call for details. Written
details had to be provided to applicants within 30 days or when a card was received, whichever was sooner.
The Federal Reserve Board was authorized to require additional disclosures if card issuers began using other kinds
of fees or charges not explicitly covered by the bill.
Required the 25 largest bank-card issuers and 125 other card issuers to disclose the same information to the Fed,
which was required to compile and publish it twice a year.
Pre-empted states from requiring disclosures beyond those specified by the bill or the Fed.
Required notice to cardholders who subscribed to credit-life insurance coverage when a change in insurance
underwriter resulted in “any increase” in the insurance rate, which typically was applied to outstanding card balances, or
in a “substantial decrease” in coverage.
Conference
Conferees reached agreement Oct. 5, ending a 10-month wait to act on the relatively non-controversial measure.
The House had originally passed the bill in October 1987, and the Senate had done likewise in December. But
House and Senate Banking Committee members had been tied up with a comprehensive measure on bank powers
(S 1886, HR 5094), and action on the credit-card bill was set aside in the interim. (Bank powers, p. 230)
Conferees were finally appointed the week of Oct. 3, and they met Oct. 5, agreeing in short order to a compromise
that had been mostly arranged long in advance.

While the bill sought to make banks, department stores and other card issuers provide consumers with timely,
understandable and complete details about their cards' terms, it put no limit on the amount of interest on
outstanding balances that card issuers might charge.
House sponsor Charles E. Schumer, D-N.Y., had once favored an interest-rate cap. But in the face of strong
opposition from the industry, Schumer retreated and pressed instead for stiff disclosure requirements that he and
colleague Doug Barnard Jr., D-Ga., said would allow consumers to choose cards with the most favorable terms.
Absent a rate cap, banks said they had no opposition to a disclosure bill. In fact they supported the measure, in
large part because its provisions would supersede the differing disclosure requirements of about a dozen states,
setting a single national standard.
Most consumer advocates praised the bill, but neither they nor the banks won on all points.
The banks, for instance, opposed a provision that they said would limit their ability to switch among firms that
underwrote so-called credit-life insurance — policies that cardholders could buy to pay off their balances should they
die. Consumer groups were unhappy with a flat preemption of all state disclosure laws, even though bill sponsors
said the national disclosure standard would go as far as any existing state law.
The Senate adopted the conference report Oct. 11 by voice vote and with no discussion. The House cleared the
measure by an overwhelming 342-5 vote Oct. 20, also with out debate. (Vote 451, p. 134-H)
