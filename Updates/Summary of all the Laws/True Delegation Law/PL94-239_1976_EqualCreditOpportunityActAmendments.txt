Equal Credit Opportunity
An article from CQ Almanac 1976
The House and Senate March 9 approved the conference report on a bill (HR 6516) outlawing credit discrimination on the basis
of age, race, color, religion or national origin, clearing it for the President. President Ford signed it (PL 94 - 239) March 23.
The measure amended the 1974 Equal Credit Opportunity Act (PL 93-495), which banned credit discrimination because of sex or
marital status.
HR 6516 was passed by the House June 3, 1975, by voice vote. (1975 Almanac p. 582)
As reported by the Senate Banking, Housing and Urban Affairs Committee Jan. 21 (S Rept 94–589) the bill included a number of
strengthening provisions not in the House version. The Senate passed its bill Feb. 2 routinely by voice vote.
The conference report on HR 6516 (H Rept 94–8731), filed March 4, conformed to the Senate version of the bill in most areas
where the two versions differed. Major changes included the approval of these Senate provisions:
A requirement that lenders notify credit applicants of their final decision and provide the reasons for rejections on request. The
conferees modified some of the details of the requirement and added an exemption from written explanations for creditors who act on
150 or fewer applications a year. That was intended to “relieve the very small credit grantor from the burden of preparing formal written
documents,” the conferees said.
An increase in the recovery ceiling applied to class action credit discrimination suits, from the existing limit of $100,000 or 1 per cent of
a creditor's net worth, to $500,000 or 1 per cent of net worth, whichever was less. The conferees dropped a House provision stipulating
that in order to award damages in civil actions under the discrimination law, a court must find that a creditor acted “willfully.” They also
adopted an amendment offered by a House conferee to permit creditors challenged in court to rely on interpretations of the law
provided by the staff of the Federal Reserve Board under authorization of the whole board.
A prohibition on credit discrimination based on receipt of public assistance payments or exercise of rights under the consumer credit
law.
Permission for creditors to include information about an applicant's age in a government-approved “credit scoring” system designed to
determine creditworthiness. The conferees dropped a Senate provision that would have allowed such systems to consider whether an
applicant received income from public assistance benefits.
Creation of a single Consumer Advisory Council to advise the Federal Reserve Board on equal credit and truth-inlending matters,
instead of a separate consumer credit advisory body recommended by the House bill.
Authorization for the Federal Reserve Board to exempt any class of business credit transaction from the law if the board decided such
an exemption would not result in illegal credit discrimination.
The Senate adopted the report by voice vote, the House by a vote of 384–3. (Vote 65, p. 22-H)
Provisions
As signed, HR 6516:
Outlawed discrimination in the granting of consumer credit based on age, race, color, religion or national origin; or because an
applicant receives public assistance or has brought a credit discrimination enforcement action.
Allowed creditors to extend credit to special groups such as elderly citizens or young couples under “affirmative action” programs
without violating the discrimination law.
Allowed creditors to include the age of a credit applicant in a statistically valid “credit scoring system” as long as advanced age is not
assigned a negative value.

Required creditors to notify applicants when a final decision had been reached on their applications, and to provide reasons for
rejections if requested. Creditors who act on 150 or fewer applications a year were not required to provide written explanations.
Established a Consumer Advisory Council to advise the Federal Reserve Board on all consumer credit matters.
Authorized the Federal Reserve Board to exempt from the law any class of business credit transaction if the board decided such an
exemption would not result in illegal credit discrimination.
Permitted states to enforce their own credit discrimination laws as long as they do not conflict with the federal law; state laws could be
more stringent.
Increased the amount recoverable under credit discrimination class action suits to $500,000 or 1 per cent of the creditor's net worth,
whichever is less. (The existing ceiling was the lesser of $100,000 or 1 per cent of net worth.)
Permitted creditors challenged in court to rely on interpretations of the law provided by the staff of the Federal Reserve Board under
authorization of the whole board.
