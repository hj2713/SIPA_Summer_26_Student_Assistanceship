Final Action
Congress cleared legislation reauthorizing housing and community development programs but dropped a controversial new
program that would have subsidized mortgages for apartments for middle-income renters.
House-Senate conferees reached agreement on the omnibus measure (S 2719) Sept. 24 and filed their report (H Rept 96–1420)
Sept. 26. The Senate adopted the conference report by voice vote Sept. 30. The House adopted it by voice vote the same day,
clearing the bill for the president. It was signed into law (PL 96 - 399) Oct. 8.
The legislation authorized a myriad of housing and community development programs, revised Federal Housing Administration
(FHA) mortgage rates and provided limited protection to condominium and cooperative owners.
For assisted housing, the single most costly item, Congress authorized $1.5 billion in fiscal 1981 contract authority and $31.2
billion in long-term budget authority for public housing and Section 8 low-income rent subsidy programs. The money would fund
282,000 additional units of housing.
The administration had requested $33.2 billion in budget authority, which the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) said would have
financed 283,000 low-income units.
The funding was nearly identical to the amount authorized by the House, but the House bill also would have financed the 32,000
units of middle-income housing dropped in conference. The Senate had proposed spending $29.9 billion in budget authority for
low-income assisted housing and had eliminated a 124,000-unit middle-income program from its version of the bill.
Also included in the final legislation was an $11.8 billion authorization for fiscal 1981–83 for Community Development Block
Grants and $675 million per year for Urban Development Action Grants (UDAG).
The administration had requested $3.95 billion for block grants in fiscal 1981, but Congress approved only $3.81 billion. However,
it met the administration's request for UDAG.
The legislation also revised the Section 235 homeownership assistance program to provide aid to middle-income home owners
and updated the 1974 Brooke-Cranston Emergency Home Purchase Act, which was designed to help the housing industry in
times of housing recession.

Committee Action
The recommendation of both banking committees that federal subsidies be provided for rental housing for middle-income families
was a substantial shift in housing policy.
The bills (S 2719 — S Rept 96–736; HR 7262 — H Rept 96–979) were reported by both the Senate and House Banking
committees May 15.
Income limits ranged from $20,000 for a family of four in the Senate measure to $30,000 in the House. One member estimated
about 80 percent of American families might qualify.
Both plans called for mortgage subsidies to allow developers to produce new or substantially rehabilitated multifamily housing for
middle-income tenants. The theory was that by reducing the developer's costs, production would be stimulated and the savings
could be passed along in the form of lower rents.
The middle-income housing was to be financed by shifting funds in the low-income rental housing program called Section 8. Sixty
percent of the Section 8 money currently was used for new construction, which had been criticized as very expensive. The
committee bills emphasized housing the poor in less costly existing housing instead, with the savings poured into the new

Following is a summary of key provisions in the bills:
Authorization. The House committee recommended $39.987 billion in fiscal 1981 budget authority for assisted housing,
community development and a host of mortgage insurance programs. The Senate recommended $36.860 billion in fiscal 1981.
The administration request was $40.3 billion.
Community Development Block Grants. In contrast to previous fights over the allocation formula, the 1980 program sparked
little controversy. (1977 Almanac p. 126)
Both panels recommended extending block grants for three years, through fiscal year 1983. The House committee authorized
$3.81 billion for fiscal 1981, $3.96 billion for fiscal 1982 and $4.11 billion for fiscal 1982.
The Senate committee recommended $3.95 billion for fiscal 1981, $4.1 billion in fiscal 1982 and $4.25 billion in fiscal 1983.
Enacted in 1974 (PL 93-383), the block grant program consolidated formerly separate neighborhood development programs.
(1974 Almanac p. 345)
Urban Development Action Grants. Both committees authorized $675 million a year for fiscal 1981–83 for the UDAG program,
which was used to spur private investment in severely distressed cities.
Rehabilitation Loans. Both panels raised the loan limits for Section 312 loans to rehabilitate single-family homes. The House
committee voted to raise the ceiling to $32,000 from $27,000, the Senate to $35,000. The House panel approved $183 million for
fiscal 1981, and the Senate committee recommended $124 million in fiscal 1981, $129 million in fiscal 1982 and $134 million in
fiscal 1983.
Both also approved making loans available for congregate and single-room occupancy housing.
Energy. Both recommended community energy conservation and planning programs. The Senate committee authorized for fiscal
1981 $75 million for a separate community energy block grant program. Communities would have to draw up a three-year plan to
survey local energy needs. The House panel allowed a community to use its community block grant money to fund a range of
energy-related activities such as insulating low-income homes.
Housing. For the Section 235 homeownership assistance program, both committees raised the limits on a subsidized mortage
for a low-income home buyer to $40,000 from $32,000.
The House committee also recommended opening up the program to middle-income home buyers as a way of shoring up the
declining home building industry. The House panel recommended spending $123 million out of $165 million of already
appropriated funds for Section 235 mortgages aid for more affluent families.
Currently, families with incomes up to 95 percent of the area median could qualify for mortgages up to $32,000 with interest rates
as low as 4 percent.
Those families could still qualify for 4 percent mortgages, but the House would use the bulk of Section 235 funds to extend the
program to families earning up to 120 percent of the area median. A home owner would be required to pay 20 percent of his or
her income toward monthly mortgage and tax payments. For these more affluent families, the federal government would pick up
the difference in monthly charges between an 8 percent mortgage and the current Federal Housing Administration (FHA) insured
mortgage rate. A cap of $50,000 would be placed on the amount of each mortgage.
The committee also updated the 1974 Brooke-Cranston mortgage subsidy program that would be activated in periods of housing
recession. It raised Brooke-Cranston's interest rate and home price ceilings to reflect prevailing market conditions.
Currently the HUD secretary could provide 7.5 percent mortgages for homes costing up to $48,000. The committee
recommended setting mortgage limits at $60,000 and purchase price limits at 105 percent of the mortgage cap. The housing
secretary was given authority to set the interest rate. The Senate April 22 approved similar Brooke-Cranston revisions as part of
S 2177.
Public Housing. Of the total $1.395 billion in fiscal 1981 contract authority recommended by the Senate panel for Section 8 and
conventional public housing, $321.3 million was set aside for construction of new public housing units. The House committee
authorized a total of $1.648 billion in fiscal 1981 contract authority, of which $261 million would be reserved for public housing.
Both committees authorized $862 million in fiscal 1981 for public housing subsidies.
The House committee authorized $100 million in fiscal 1981 for a comprehensive program of improvements and energy
conservation.
Tenant Rights. The Senate committee endorsed a broad-based tenants' bill of rights, designed to protect purchasers of
condominium units and families displaced by condominium conversions.
Condominium sellers would be barred from using any fraudulent or deceptive marketing practices. The developer would have to
give tenants at least 120 days notice before he forced them to vacate. Tenants would have 90 days, following notice, to purchase
their units. If they decided not to buy, they would have 30 more days to vacate.

Senate Floor Action
The Senate overwhelmingly rejected the proposal to subsidize apartments for middle-income families, voting instead to pour the
money into more low-income housing.
The bill (S 2719) approved by a vote of 63–4 June 21 also added tougher income restrictions to the Section 8 rental assistance
program for low-income families. The change limited the aid to the very poor. (Vote 239, p. 35-S)
The drive to cut the plan to subsidize mortgages for 124,000 apartments for middle-income families was led by Jake Garn, R-
Utah who said it was “our first responsibility to assist those families most in need of federal subsidies.”
Garn, ranking minority member of the Senate Banking Committee, offered an amendment to shift the $2.4 billion in budget
authority for middle-income housing to low-income housing. The Senate accepted the amendment by a vote of 47–23. (Vote 235,
p. 35-S)
Critics argued that Garn's plan would actually mean less housing for the poor than originally proposed, cutting the units from
286,000 to 255,000.
A number of liberal interest groups sided with Garn. The National Low Income Housing Coalition, the Urban Coalition, the
National Urban League and Americans for Democratic Action (ADA) all fought hard to divert the funds to low-income housing.
Programs Reauthorized
S 2719 authorized $29.9 billion in budget authority and $1.419 billion in contract authority for assisted and public housing
programs for fiscal 1981. That compared to the $1.5 billion in contract authority requested by the administraton and $1.4 billion
authorized by Congress last year.
Included in the bill was $36.8 billion for housing and community development programs, $3.7 billion for rural housing programs
and $61.6 million for federal surveys of flood prone areas.
The Banking Committee May 15 reported a separate rural housing measure (S 2711 S Rept 96–724) and flood study bill (S 2716
— S Rept 96–716), which were added on the floor to the omnibus housing package.
The bill extended the Community Development Block Grant and Urban Development Action Grant (UDAG) programs for three
years without major change, broadened the Section 235 low-income homeownership assistance program to provide aid to
moderate-income families and updated the 1974 Brooke-Cranston emergency home purchase act.
It also delayed for up to two years application of federal building energy performance standards called for by the 1976 Energy
Conservation and Production Act (PL 94-383) to force builders to provide energy-efficient commercial and residential buildings.
Environmentalists opposed extending the deadline from Aug. 14 to Aug. 1, 1982. But the industry and the Department of Energy
said time was needed to solve technical problems.
Middle-Income Housing
Under the committee plan, a family of four with an income of 130 percent of the median income in its area, or roughly $20,000 a
year, could have qualified for the housing. The plan would have provided 43 percent new construction, 57 percent existing
housing.
But Garn and others called it an unworkable, unfair shift in housing policy.
Under Garn's amendment, shifting the $2.4 billion in budget authority intended for the new housing would help support 255,000
units of low-income rental housing. The ratio would be restored to 60 percent new housing, 40 percent existing.
Garn complained that the committee plan would take money from the poor and that the middle-income proposal would “do little or
nothing to reduce the costs of assisted housing programs.” A recent CBO study showed that the long-term costs of providing new
housing under Section 8 could go as high as $710,000 per unit over the life of the contract.
But Banking Committee Chairman William Proxmire, D-Wis. countered that the Garn proposal would “result in less housing
construction [and] less low-income housing.” Proxmire said the committee plan would provide 286,000 low-income units for the
same price as the 255,000 units under the Garn proposal.
Income Ceiling
The Senate considered several plans to lower income levels for eligibility under the Section 8 rental assistance program.
Currently, a family of four with an income 80 percent of the area median qualified.
Under Section 8, families whose incomes fell below 80 percent of the median paid between 15 and 30 percent of their incomes in
rent. The government paid landlords the difference between the family payment and the market rent.

Mortgage Disclosure Act
The 1975 Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA) (PL 94-200), which expired June 28, 1980, required savings and loan
associations and banks to make annual disclosures of the number, location and dollar amount of their mortgage and home
improvement loans.
The Banking panel had recommended making the law permanent because it had become a major tool in the fight against
housing discrimination. But Garn proposed letting the law expire on Sept. 30, 1985. Garn argued that the act's reporting
requirements meant Congress should review it periodically.
Garn's amendment failed on a 37–37 vote. But the Senate voted 38–36 to approve a motion by Jacob K. Javits, D-N.Y. to
reconsider the vote and the Garn amendment was defeated 36–38. (Vote 232, p. 34-S; votes 233 and 234, p. 35-S)
Brooke-Cranston, Section 235. The Senate approved by voice vote an amendment by Harrison A. Williams, D-N.J. attaching the
provisions of S 2177 passed April 22.
The measure included an update of the 1974 Brooke-Cranston act (PL 93-449). Current law authorized the HUD secretary to
provide 7.5 percent mortgages to home buyers who agreed to buy homes costing no more than $48,000.
Under the Senate bill, the subsidized mortgage could be no more than 3 percent below the prevailing market rate for single-
family home mortgages and the purchase price would be pegged at 90 percent of the mean purchase price in a given area.
In addition, the Senate approved a proposal to broaden eligibility for the Section 235 homeownership assistance program which
subsidized mortgages. The Senate allowed moderate-income families to qualify for the program.
Under the bill, $135 million of $165 million of already appropriated funds would support the moderate-income Section 235
program. The remaining funds would be used to support 4,000 to 7,000 units under the low-income portion of Section 235,
according to HUD estimates.
The top price of a home that could be purchased under the moderate-income portion of the program was raised from $38,000 to
$60,000, or 80 percent of the average new house price for an area, whichever was higher. Interest rates on mortgages, insured
by FHA, were hiked from 4 percent to 11 percent.
The existing Section 235 program served only those home buyers with incomes at 95 percent of the area median income or
below, but the Senate bill contained no income restrictions for the moderate-income initiative.
House Floor Action
The House authorization of $38.5 billion included the federally subsidized rental housing for middle-income families and was
passed Aug. 22 by a 291–43 vote. (Vote 428, p. 126-H)
HR 7262 extended the community development and UDAG programs for three years and authorized the revamped subsidized
housing programs for one year. Thomas L. Ashley, D-Ohio chairman of the Housing and Community Development Subcommittee,
said the measure provided for 352,500 housing units for fiscal 1981, about 50,000 more than projected in President Carter's
budget.
According to a subcommittee staffer, the bill provided a total of $1.536 billion in annual contract authority for five federally
assisted housing programs under Title II of the bill and $31.229 billion in budget authority.
The annual contract authority set a limit on the amount the government might obligate pursuant to a contract in any given year.
Budget authority represented the aggregate liability over a number of years.
Middle-income program
Under the new middle-income housing program, the government would subsidize the developer's mortgage costs down to an
average of 7.5 percent.
Middle-income renters were defined as those whose incomes do not exceed 150 percent of the median income of their area.
The demonstration project for fiscal 1981 included a maximum of 40,000 subsidized units, with 32,000 for middle-income families
and 8,000 reserved for lower-income residents in the Section 8 rental assistance program.
By a 239–162 vote, the House adopted an amendment by Chalmers P. Wylie, R-Ohio a subcommittee member, barring federal
funds for the new units in areas with local rent control laws. Some 200 cities, including New York City and Washington, D.C., had
such laws. The committee exempted the new program from rent control laws. (Vote 421, p. 124-H)
Wylie argued that a private developer's profits would be limited by the rent control laws but a developer participating in the new
plan and free of the controls would have a guaranteed return on his investment.
Opponents said the provision would not lead rent-control cities to repeal their ordinances and would mean that many areas would
not be eligible for the new program.

Other new provisions
HR 7262 included three other new elements:
A $100 million program to improve the physical condition and management of public housing.
Revision of the Section 235 mortgage subsidy home-ownership program to stimulate the home building industry by increasing the
mortgage limits for single-family homes to $47,500 in most areas and $55,000 in high-cost areas, up from $38,000 and $44,000,
respectively.
In addition, it established a one-year special program to boost home building by broadening the mortgage limits and eligibility further.
That included raising income limits of eligible families to 120 percent of the area median, from 95 percent and increasing mortgage
limits for single-family homes to $50,000 in most areas and $60,000 in high-cost areas. Funds would be restricted to 75 percent of
already appropriated funds, about $123 million, with authority to expire in Sept. 30, 1981.
The bill also allowed the government to recapture its subsidy when a home was sold to an owner not eligible for the Section 235
program.
Creation of a temporary mortgage assistance program in which the HUD secretary could make mortgage payments on behalf of a
home owner in default because of circumstances beyond the home owner's control. No payments could be made unless the secretary
determined there was a “reasonable prospect” that the mortgagor could resume payments and any federal funds spent must be repaid
by the owner. An 18-month limit on the payments could be extended in unusual circumstances.
Over the objection of the bill's sponsors, the House adopted by voice vote an amendment by John J. Duncan, R-Tenn. to exclude
Social Security benefit increases effective after May 1980 in considering eligibility for subsidized housing.
Ashley, who led the opposition, argued that the amendment “gives special treatment to one group of people simply because of
their source of income.” But Duncan said the elderly were being penalized when the higher benefits pushed incomes above the
limits for assisted housing.
Floor action
Some 27 amendments were considered during three days of debate on the bill, most of them in Title II.
Title I. Community and Neighborhood Development and Conservation
A major controversy involved a Wylie amendment aimed at preventing delays in a UDAG project while historic preservation
agencies decided whether a building within the project was in fact worthy of preservation and protection. His compromise created
a 90-day, two-step review process and was adopted by voice vote.
Under current law, potentially historic structures did not have to be identified in a grant application, there was no time limit on an
historic preservation review and it was not clear whether HUD could proceed with an application while the review was under way.
Under Wylie's amendment, an applicant for a UDAG grant must identify structures that might qualify for historic preservation and
state the effect of the project on those structures. The appropriate state historic society would have 45 days to comment on the
historic nature of any such structures.
After receiving the state assessment, the applicant would submit the UDAG request to the National Register of Historic Places for
comment within 45 days.
HUD could approve the grant while the review was under way but no funds could be released until the reviews were completed.

Amendments adopted included:
By Lawrence Coughlin, R-Pa. as amended by Robert A. Young, D-Mo. to prohibit the HUD secretary from refusing to consider a
proposed subsidized housing project because it would be located in an area with a large minority or low-income population, by voice
vote.
By Clarence E. Miller, R-Ohio to restrict the authority of the secretary to increase rent limits for Section 8 low-income rental
assistance, 204–183. (Vote 423, p. 126-H)
By Morris K. Udall, D-Ariz. providing that the existing variance on insulation standards granted to Florida and Arizona remain in effect
until  the Department of Energy issued permanent standards, by voice vote.

Title V. Rural Housing
By a 230–108 vote the House adopted an amendment by M. Caldwell Butler, R-Va. to strike a provision involving development of
low-income housing in rural areas. Butler's amendment prohibited the retroactive application of a provision enacted in 1979 that
barred prepayment of a federal loan. (Vote 427, p. 126-H)
Butler said some developers of rural rental units for low-income families had paid off their loans and converted the units to other
uses. Butler said allowing the prepayment bar to apply retroactively amounted to “changing the rules in the middle of the game.”
After final passage, the House adopted the Senate bill as the vehicle for conference after amending the measure to contain the
House language.
Final Action
Only frantic last-minute negotiations between Senate Banking Committee Chairman William Proxmire, D-Wis. and key House
Banking Committee members averted a total collapse of the bill, sources said.
At issue was a provision making the 1975 Home Mortgage Disclosure Act permanent. The Senate had updated HMDA but the
House had not. The anti-redlining act required savings and loan associations and banks to make annual disclosures of the
number, location and dollar amount of their mortgage and home improvement loans.
But despite the House conferees' apparent concession to the Senate on HMDA, some lawmakers remained troubled over the
permanent law provision.
Wylie, a House conferee, indicated he would try to strike the HMDA provisions from S 2719 when the conference report reached
the House floor. That would raise the possibility of Senate rejection of the report and a return to conference.
However, after last-minute negotiations, Proxmire agreed to offer a concurrent resolution (S Con Res 131) to end HMDA in five
years.
After the Senate approved the conference report by voice vote it approved the concurrent resolution Sept. 30. The House
followed suit, approving the conference report by voice vote, and then agreed to the Senate resolution the same day.
Middle-income Plan
A key issue that threatened to deadlock the conferees was the House-passed program to subsidize mortgages for apartments for
middle-income renters.
Helping to avert the deadlock was Proxmire's last-minute change of heart. He had been expected to side with the House
conferees to preserve the initiative. But scathing press reports castigating attention to middle-class families earning up to $30,000
a year influenced Proxmire.
“I think as The New York Times said, we need [the middle-income program] like a hole in the head,” Proxmire said during debate
on the fiscal 1981 HUD appropriations bill.
With Proxmire's support withdrawn, the program was pronounced dead by its House supporters.
In exchange for dropping the middle-income proposal, the Senate agreed to back off from its stand lowering to 65 percent, from
80 percent, the average median income of families qualifying for Section 8 aid.
The Senate also agreed to go along with the House-passed $31.2 billion in budget authority for assisted housing. Conferees
agreed to divide the total number of Section 8 and public housing units equally between new and existing units.
Other Conference Issues
Conferees agreed to adopt a compromise that set the price of homes insured under the 235 program at no higher than 82
percent of the FHA rate. Home owners would have to pay between 20 and 25 percent of their incomes toward monthly mortgage
and tax payments. The government would pick up the difference in monthly charges between a 9.5 percent mortgage and the
current FHA rate.
Families with incomes equaling 130 percent of the median income in their area could apply for the low-interest mortgages.
They also modified Senate-passed language that would have provided a tenants' bill of rights, to protect condominium and
cooperative purchasers and families displaced by conversions.
Instead of the broad-ranging federal protections originally proposed, the conferees agreed to shift the burden for regulating
conversions to state and local governments.
