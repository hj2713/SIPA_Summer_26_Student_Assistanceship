Background
President Eisenhower Jan. 19 in his fiscal 1960 Budget Message served notice he thought it was time for the
Federal Government to stage a partial withdrawal on the housing  front.
The recommendation which caused the most furore concerned the future of the urban renewal program. Mr.
Eisenhower urged Congress to revise the urban renewal laws so as to “require states and cities to assume a
gradually increasing share of the expense of buying and improving the land and of other net project costs.” He
recommended that the Federal share of the cost of an urban renewal project be reduced, in stages, from two-thirds
to one-half.
President Eisenhower's recommendations, which alarmed the Nation's mayors and brought protests from such
pressure groups as the American Municipal Assn. and U.S. Conference of Mayors, came as the urban renewal
program entered its 10th year.
The program was authorized in the Housing  Act of 1949 (PL 171, 81st Congress). The Act declared it was in
the “general welfare” for Congress to clear away slums, “thus contributing to the development and redevelopment of
communities and to the advancement of the growth, wealth and security of the Nation.”
The program is run by the Urban Renewal Administration within the Housing  and Home Finance Agency. The
URA commits the available money to cities that apply, after checking to see if they meet such Federal require-
ments as having a workable plan for renewing a slum area. Cities may borrow money from the Federal Government
for planning a slum clearance project and clearing the site.
Once the former slum site is ready for new buildings, the city usually sells the land to a private developer. The
money the developer pays the city for the site is subtracted from the total amount the city spent on the slum
clearance project. The resulting figure is the city's net cost, or loss. The Federal Government pays the city for two-

Democratic leaders in Congress, strongly opposed to President Eisenhower's pleas for less, rather than more,
Federal help for housing , made plans early in the 1959 session to pass a broad bill  similar to the one
which failed to pass the House in the closing hours of the 85th Congress. (1958 Almanac p. 225) Because the 1958
elections had provided them with 2 to 1 margins over the Republicans in both the Senate and House, the
Democratic leaders had reason to feel confident about getting a broad housing bill  through Congress.
Chairman John J. Sparkman (D Ala.) of the Senate Banking and Currency Housing  Subcommittee Jan. 9
introduced a bill  (S 57) similar to the one which foundered in 1958. A decidedly more liberal measure (S 193)
was introduced Jan. 12 by Sens. Joseph S. Clark (D Pa.) and John F. Kennedy (D Mass.). A bill  whose scope
was in between those two Senate measures was introduced Jan. 15 by Chairman Albert Rains (D Ala.) of the House
Banking and Currency Housing  Subcommittee (HR 2357).
The Democratic proposals brought warnings from Republicans that President Eisenhower would veto any bill
which would upset the balance of his fiscal 1960 budget. After a White House meeting Jan. 20, Senate Minority
Leader Everett McKinley Dirksen (R Ill.) said the President was determined to “dampen the inflationary fever.”
The Administration's bills (S 65, which applied only to fiscal 1959 emergency needs, and S 612, which carried
longer-range housing  proposals) were introduced in January.

Urban Renewal
The Housing  Act of 1949 (1949 Almanac p. 274) authorized the Federal Government to spend money to help
communities clear away their slums. The city clears a slum area and then sells the site to a private developer who
puts up new homes and shops. The Federal Government pays the city two-thirds of its loss on the deal. For
example, if the city spent $1 million to buy up and clear the site but only received $500,000 from the developer, the
Federal Government would give the city two-thirds of $500,000, or $333,000. The demand for this Federal money
has outrun the supply in recent years. The city mayors, through such organizations as the American Municipal Assn.
and U.S. Conference of Mayors, said the 86th Congress should authorize in 1959 an additional $600 million a year
for 10 years.
Other features of the existing urban renewal program included Federal money to help cities plan slum clearance
projects (shown as “urban planning” on the accompanying chart), temporary loans until the city gets private
financing of its project (urban renewal advances), and Federal payments to move people displaced by slum
clearance projects (relocation payments). The Senate bill  provided a new feature: graduate scholarships for
students desirous of studying housing . Following are the three proposed urban renewal provisions:
ADMINISTRATION – $1.55 billion, which included a $100 million emergency fund at the disposal of the President
and the $100 million recommended to subsidize slum clearance projects for the balance of fiscal 1959. The
remaining $1.35 billion would be spent over six years at this rate: $250 million for fiscal 1960, 1961 and 1962; $200
million for fiscal 1963, 1964 and 1965. The Federal share of the community's loss on a slum clearance project
would be reduced gradually from two-thirds to one-half. The reductions would be to 60 percent in fiscal 1961; 55
percent in fiscal 1962; 50 percent in fiscal 1963, 1964 and 1965.
$10 million for urban planning.
SENATE – $2.1 billion over six years for slum clearance projects. The outlay would be $350 million a year in fiscal
1959 through 1964.
$10 million for urban planning.

Public Housing
The U.S. Housing  Act of 1937 authorized the Federal Government to help localities build low-rent housing
units for persons of low income. Basically, the Government's aid is in two steps. First, theGovernment loans the
local public housing  authority money which is repaid when it sells construction bonds on the project to private
investors. Second, the Government pays that portion of the indebtedness that the managers of the public housing
 project cannot meet through their rental income. This latter subsidy was limited by the 1949 Housing  Act to
$336 million a year. The 1949 Act said no more than 810,000 public housing  units could be subsidized. Actual
construction since then has fallen far short of that total. At the close of fiscal 1959, only 241,145 units were
completed and about 101,500 were under contract.
ADMINISTRATION – No new units authorized.
SENATE – Authorized subsidization of an additional 35,000 public housing  units from July 1, 1959 through
June 30, 1963 and extended deadline of previous authorizations. The net effect would be authority for the Federal
Government to subsidize about 45,000 units between enactment of the bill  and June 30,1963. Republicans said
the Federal help to pay off the construction bonds over a 40–48 year period would come to $883 million.
HOUSE – Authorized 35,000 units a year until the 810,000 authorized in 1949 were completed, but said the Federal
subsidy could not exceed $336 million in any one year. The Democratic majority of the House Banking and Currency
Committee said this money ceiling would limit additional construction to 145,000 units, or 35,000 a year for four
years. The Republican minority said the authorization would allow 190,000 additional units and would cost the
Government $3.7 billion over 40–48 years. Democrats replied this should not be included as new obligations
because the units were authorized by the 1949 Act.
College Housing
The Housing  Act of 1950 authorized the Federal Government to make direct loans to colleges and universities
which could not obtain adequate financing elsewhere. The loans, principally for the construction of college
dormitories, are repayable over 40 years at low interest rates (approximately 3 percent). As of Dec. 31, 1958, $925
million had been authorized since the program started. There were applications on hand on Dec. 31, 1958, totaling
$52.9 million, butonly $3.2 million wasleft in the fund.
ADMINISTRATION – $200 million in fiscal 1959 for dormitory construction loans. Beyond 1959, the Eisen hower
Administration in a separate proposal (S 1017, HR 4267) recommended Federal subsidies to help colleges pay off
their construction bonds, both on dormitories and other facilities such as classrooms and laboratories. The Federal
grants would be limited to $500 million over 20 years.
SENATE – $400 million for college housing  loans with $50 million reserved for nurse and intern dormitories and
$50 million for such service facilities as student unions and cafeterias.
$125 million in loans for such academic facilities as classrooms and libraries and another $125 million to guarantee
private loans on such construction. These would be new programs.
HOUSE – $400 million in college housing  loans with $40 million reserved for nurse and intern dormitories and
$40 million for other service facilities.
Cooperative Housing
The Federal Housing  Administration encourages banks to make big loans to cooperatives by agreeing to
guarantee loans of up to 90 percent of the cost of the project (95 percent where half the tenants are veterans).
Consumer cooperative apartments are ones built, through contracting, by the same people who will live in them.
The future tenants thus can tailor the apartment to their needs and also have the advantages of liberal financing.
The other type of cooperatives, sales type, are constructed by builders under FHA special financing advantages and
then sold. The only financial obligation in this program was authorized in the HOUSE bill . It would authorize the
Federal National Mortgage Assn. to buy $75 million worth of cooperative housing  mortgages, $25 million upon
enactment of the bill  and $50 million the following July 1. Half of the $75 million would go for consumer type
cooperatives and the other half for builder-sponsored sales type.
Elderly Housing
Like the cooperative program, the Government provides incentive for construction of housing  for the elderly by
offering liberal mortgage terms. The HOUSE bill  authorized, in addition tothis, direct Federal loans to nonprofit
corporations building apartments for persons 62 years old or more. A labor union, for instance, could obtain a direct
Federal loan for 98 percent of the cost of an apartment building it wanted to construct for retired workers. The union
would pay the loan back over 50 years at 31/2 percent interest. The House Banking and Currency Committee said

During the Korean War, Congress gave communities money to help build such facilities as hospitals for the influx of

Floor Action
The Senate Feb. 5 passed S 57 on a 60–28 roll-call vote. (For voting see chart p. 401)
By its sponsors' estimates, the measure would have provided $2.7 billion over six years for Federal housing
programs. But after adoption of last-minute compromises, Democratic and Republican leaders estimated it would
exceed President Eisenhower's housing  requests for fiscal 1960 by only $35 million in actual expenditures.
Debate on whether it would upset the President's precariously balanced fiscal 1960 budget ran all through the
discussions on the bill .
The sharpest single battle was over raising interest rates on GI loans from 4.75 to 5.25 percent. Sens. A.S. Mike
Monroney (D Okla.) and Albert Gore (DTenn.) contended such a hike would amount to “legislating inflation.” Their
amendment to freeze the rates was rejected, 27–58. (For final action on interest rates and direct veterans' loans,
see story p. 257.)
Majority Leader Lyndon B. Johnson (D Texas), after a meeting with President Eisenhower Feb. 4, sponsored an
amendment which eliminated the $125 million in S 57 to guarantee college loans and reduced the college loan fund
from $400 million to $300 million. Johnson said Feb. 5: “We think we ought to go as far as possible to meet the
Administration half-way.”
Amendments to substitute the Administration's housing  proposals, in toto and in part, were defeated.

Conference
The Senate June 22, by a 56–31 roll-call vote, and the House June 23, on a 241–177 roll call, adopted the
conference report on the Housing  Act of 1959 (S 57). (For voting, see charts p. 418, 372) The action sent the 
bill  to the President. Two of his advisers, Budget Director Maurice H. Stans and Norman P. Mason,
administrator of the Housing  and Home Finance Agency, said they would recommend a veto of the bill
despite the fact that House-Senate conferees had cut the total cost below the original Senate and House figures.
The cuts were described as a “block that veto” attempt.
The Administration and the Democratic backers of S 57 differed on the immediate and ultimate costs of the bill .
Conferees said it contained $1,375,400,000 in obligational authority compared to Administration requests for
$1,760,000,000 and Senate and House authorizations of $2,551,700,000 and $2,100,100,000, respectively. The
HHFA said the bill  would obligate the Government to spend $2,249,900,000 – $874.5 million of which it
estimated as the 40-year cost of the public housing  units provided. The bill 's backers did not include a
public housing  estimate in their figures.
As to the bill 's impact on the Federal budget in fiscal 1960, Democrats estimated spending at between $28
million and $69 million, the HHFA said it would come to $75,340,000. Some but not all of the total would depend
upon appropriations. Conferees had struck from the bill  a House amendment sponsored by Albert Thomas (D
Texas) that would have made funds for Federal National Mortgage Assn. operations, urban renewal and college 
housing  available only through appropriation bills, instead of through contract authority as was current practice.
None of the Republican House conferees and none of the GOP Senate conferees except Homer E. Capehart (Ind.)
signed the conference report. The major cut made in the bill  was a reduction to $900 million over a two-year
period for urban renewal grants. The Administration had requested $1,450,000,000 over a six-year period, the
Senate provided $2.1 billion for the same time and the House provided $1.5 billion over two years. The
Administration proposed $500 million for the first two years in its original bill  (S 612).

Veto of First Bill
President Eisenhower July 7 vetoed S 57, declaring: “the bill  is extravagant and much of the spending it
authorizes is unnecessary…. Even though we have over 100,000 previously authorized public housing  units as
yet unbuilt, the bill  would authorize 190,000 more.” (Democrats said the “unbuilt” units already were assigned
and that 145,000 of the 190,000authorized were discretionary, not mandatory.)
The President criticized the urban renewal provisions; the new program for elderly persons; continuance of the
college loan program and the new one for classrooms; and the requirement that Federal National Mortgage Assn.
mortgages be sold at par. Mr. Eisenhower said the bill  was inflationary and “would tend to substitute Federal
spending for private investment.” He said Congress' refusal to remove the ceiling on FHA's insurance authorization
would continue its “important programs on an uncertain, hand-to-mouth basis.”
Delivery of the President's message in the Senate set off more than two hours of debate. Majority Leader Johnson
said it was “unfortunate that the Congress and the country are now confronted with an all-or-nothing attitude,” and
that Congress had “gone a long way to try to meet the President in passing the bill .”
Urging Senate action on the veto, Sen. John J. Sparkman (D Ala.), chairman of the Housing  Subcommittee
which drafted S 57, said the President's message indicated he “has not been fully informed” on the bill .
Most Republicans applauded the veto and expressed the belief it could not be overridden.
Administration's New Proposal
An Administration-backed housing bill , authorizing $810 million in grants and loans, was introduced in the
Senate (S 2378) July 14.
The new bill  omitted public housing  projects, housing  for the aged, college classroom loans and other
features to which Mr. Eisenhower had objected. Major provisions called for a two-year, $600 million urban renewal
and slum clearance program, $200 million for college housing  loans, $10 million for urban planning assistance
and an immediate authorization of $5 billion for FHA mortgage insurance plus an additional $5 billion later.

other, more expensive, less popular programs that will be included in a 1960 housing bill .
Veto of Second Bill
President Eisenhower Sept. 3 vetoed the second housing bill  (S 2539) on grounds it represented “little over-all
improvement over S 57.” He said its extension of FHA insurance authority for only one year was “worse than the
earlier housing bill .”
In addition to criticizing the cost of the bill , the President said the proposed direct loan program to colleges for
classrooms and related facilities “is Federal aid to education in a highly objectionable form.”
He said there was “still time for the Congress to enact a sound housing bill ” and urged passage of a bill
that would: “(1) remove the ceiling on FHA mortgage insurance authority; (2) extend the FHA program for insurance
of property improvement loans; (3) enact reasonable authorizations for urban renewal grants and college housing
 loans and adjust the interest rate on the latter; (4) extend the voluntary home mortgage credit program; and (5)
adjust the statutory interest rate ceilings governing mortgages insured under FHA's regular rental and cooperative 
housing  programs.”
Veto Sustained
The Senate Sept. 4, on a 58–36 roll-call vote, sustained the President's veto of S 2539. The vote was five short of
the necessary two-thirds for overriding. (For voting, see chart p. 441)
Six Republicans and 52 Democrats voted to over ride; the President's veto was upheld by 27 Republicans and nine
Democrats.
Third Housing Bill  (S 2654)
Senate
COMMITTEE – Banking and Currency.
ACTION – Sept. 8 reported a third omnibus housing bill  (S 2654 – S Rept 924) designed to meet most of
President Eisenhower's objections. Unanimous approval of S 2654 came after the Committee refused to accept a
stop-gap bill  proposed by Sen. Capehart. Committee Chairman A. Willis Robertson (D Va.) said he had “positive
assurance” that President Eisenhower would sign S 2654.

The Committee made these major changes in the second bill  (S 2539) vetoed by President Eisenhower:
Extended the FHA's authority to insure mortgages indefinitely but limited its new obligations to $8 billion. (S 2539
extended the authority to Oct. 1, 1960.) Since FHA had issued letters of intent to insure mortgages amounting to
$5.2 billion, leaving less than $3 billion in new authority, it was expected to bump against the $8 billion ceiling again
in 1960.
Eliminated the $50 million college loan program that the vetoed bill  had authorized for the construction of such
non-dormitory facilities as classrooms and laboratories.
Spread out urban renewal funds over two years, with $350 million earmarked for fiscal 1960 and $300 million for
fiscal 1961. (S 2539 authorized $550 million for urban renewal upon enactment, with another $100 million to be used
at the President's discretion. S 2654 did not restore the Presidential fund.)
Floor Action
The Senate Sept. 9 passed S 2654, on an 86–7 roll-call vote, in the form reported by the Banking and Currency
Committee. (For voting, see chart p. 444)
Instead of referring the Senate-passed bill  (S 2654) to committee with the risk it might not return to the floor
before adjournment, the House Sept. 10 suspended its rules and passed the measure, unchanged, by voice vote.
This sent the third housing bill  to the President.
PROVISIONS – See p. 245.
President Signs Bill
The President Sept. 23 signed into law the third-try omnibus housing bill  (PL 86–372 ), without comment.
Immediately following enactment, the Federal Housing  Administration announced increases in the mortgage
insurance interest rates that were authorized by the bill . Interest on rental housing  went up to 5.25 percent,
as did that on management-type cooperative housing . Interest on sales-type cooperative housing  was set
at 5.75 percent.
Features
Down Payment Schedule
Builders and realtors especially welcomed the FHA down payment schedule in the Housing  Act of 1959
because it made home buying easier. The old and new schedules for one-family homes:
OLD – 3% down on first $13,500 of house value.
NEW – Same.
OLD – 15% down on amount between $13,500–$16,000.
NEW – 10% down on amount between $13,500–$18,000.
OLD – 30% down on amount between $16,000–$20,000.
NEW – 30% down on amount between $18,000–$22,500.
