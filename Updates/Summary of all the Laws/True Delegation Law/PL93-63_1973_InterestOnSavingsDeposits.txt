Interest On Savings Deposits
An article from CQ Almanac 1973

Second Extension of Interest Authority
Congress June 29 cleared S J Res 128 (PL 93 - 63 ) extending to Aug.
1, 1973, the authority of federal bank regulatory agencies to set limits on
interest rates on time and savings deposits.
On Aug. 3, Congress cleared a second bill extending the authority through
Dec. 31, 1974. (Below)
Congress June 29 approved a one-month extension of federal bank
regulatory agencies' authority to put ceilings on the interest rates paid by
banks and other financial institutions on time and savings deposits.
Approved 29 days after the interest-ceiling authority expired on June 1, S J
Res 128 renewed the authority until Aug. 1 to give House-Senate conferees
more time to resolve a dispute over legislation (HR 6370) extending the
authority to 1975.
In passing HR 6370 by a 376-4 recorded vote on May 9, the House accepted
a Banking and Currency Committee amendment forbidding mutual savings
banks in Massachusetts and New Hampshire from continuing to operate
interest-bearing accounts on which a customer could write negotiable orders
of withdrawal (NOW accounts). Before passage, the House had defeated by a
98–264 recorded teller vote an attempt to strip the provision from the bill.
(Votes 83(T), 84, p. 28-H, 30-H)
Before passing similar legislation on May 22 by a 76-0 vote, the Senate by a
43-33 roll-call vote adopted an amendment by Bill Brock (R Tenn.) prohibiting
mutual savings banks in most states from paying interest on checking
accounts but allowing mutual savings banks in Massachusetts and New
Hampshire to continue the NOW accounts that would be prohibited by the
House version. (Votes 142, 143, p. 24-S)
With conferees deadlocked on HR 6370, the interest-ceiling authority expired
on June 1. Federal regulatory agencies nevertheless warned financial institutions not to raise interest rates on time
and savings deposits above existing levels because Congress was certain to extend the ceiling.
To that end, the Senate June 28 by voice vote passed S J Res 128 (S Rept 93 –264) extending the authority to
Dec. 31, 1974. The House, however, by voice vote under suspension of the rules amended and passed S J Res
128 providing an extension only to Aug. 1.
The Senate June 29 by voice vote completed action on S J Res 128 by accepting the House amendment.
Related Development. In an effort to encourage consumer saving, and dampen consumer spending that was
fueling inflation, the Federal Reserve Board and Federal Home Loan Bank Board July 5 raised the ceilings imposed
on savings account interest rates. Ceilings on interest paid by commercial banks were raised to 5 per cent from 4
1/2 per cent, and ceilings on interest paid by savings and loan associations were raised to 5-1/4 per cent from 5 per
cent.
Second Extension of Interest Authority
Congress Aug. 3 cleared HR 6370 (PL 93 -100) extending through Dec. 31, 1974, the authority of federal bank
regulatory agencies to set flexible limits on interest rates paid on time and savings deposits.
A compromise allowing banks in Massachusetts and New Hampshire to hold interest-bearing checking accounts
cleared the way for final congressional action.

By voice votes, both the House and the Senate adopted the conference report on HR 6370 extending federal bank
regulatory agencies' authority to set flexible interest rates on time and savings deposits held by commercial banks,
savings and loan institutions and mutual savings banks.
That authority, which expired on June 1, 1973, had been extended temporarily to Aug. 1 while House and Senate
conferees on HR 6370 struggled to resolve differences over interest-bearing checking accounts-known as negotiable
order of withdrawal (NOW) accounts-currently employed by mutual savings banks in Massachusetts and New
Hampshire. (Temporary extension, above)
As passed by the House, HR 6370 outlawed NOW accounts in all states. The Senate version, however, merely
restricted their use to the two states where such accounts already were permitted.
After a two-month delay, House and Senate conferees July 31 reached a compromise permitting NOW accounts in
New Hampshire and Massachusetts to be continued but extending the right to hold them to commercial banks and
savings and loan associations, as well as to mutual savings banks, in these two states. As part of the compromise,
the conference version would end an existing exemption for Massachusetts mutual savings banks from regulation by
the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation.
In effect, the compromise allowed Massachusetts and New Hampshire to continue NOW accounts on an
experimental basis under federal supervision. In submitting proposals for revamping the nation's financial institutions
on Aug. 3, the Nixon administration recommended that NOW accounts be permitted on a national basis.
Conference Report. In their conference report on HR 6370 (H Rept 93–418) filed July 31, House and Senate
conferees agreed to Dec. 31, 1974, as a compromise date for expiration of the interest ceiling authority for bank
regulatory agencies-the Federal Reserve Board, Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation and Federal Home Loan
Bank Board. The House bill had extended the authority through Sept. 30, 1975; the Senate bill provided an
extension to June 1, 1974.
In addition to the compromise on NOW accounts the conferees approved provisions:
Permitting national banks and savings and loan associations chartered by the Federal Home Loan Bank Board to invest
in state housing corporations in the states where their headquarters were located.
Placing a moratorium on conversion of savings and loan associations from mutual to stock organizations through June
30, 1974 (with an exception for applications for conversions filed with the Federal Savings and Loan Insurance
Corporation before Senate passage of the bill on May 22).
Creating a new procedure for payment by savings and loan associations of premiums into the insurance corporation's
reserve fund.
Forbidding the levying of state and local taxes on income of federally insured out-of-state financial institutions until  Jan.
1, 1976, and directing the Advisory Commission on Inter-Governmental Relations to study the problems that would be
posed by such taxes.
Final Action. Both the House and the Senate Aug. 3 approved the report on HR 6370 by voice votes.
