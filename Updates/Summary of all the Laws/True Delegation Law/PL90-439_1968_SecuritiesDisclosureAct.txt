An article from CQ Almanac 1968
Document Outline
Background
House
Congress July 18 cleared a bill (S 510) to give increased protection to
stockholders in companies that outside groups attempted to take over through
cash tender offers or other purchases of stock. Final action came July 18
when the Senate by voice vote concurred in House amendments to the bill.
The House July 15 passed the amended version by a voice vote. The
President signed the bill July 29 (PL 90-439).
PROVISIONS—As cleared by Congress, S 510:
Required a person or group making a tender offer to purchase a substantial
amount of securities  of a corporation to disclose information that would
be helpful to stockholders in deciding whether or not to sell their securities
. Specified that disclosure  was required in cases where purchase of
the securities  would result in the person or group owning more than 10
percent of the corporation's securities
Required the Securities  and Exchange Commission (SEC) to specify the
exact information to be disclosed but provided that it should cover the identity,
background and plans of the purchaser and sources of funds. The identity of
a bank providing credit could be kept confidential.
Permitted stockholders who accepted the purchase offer to withdraw their
acceptance during the seven days after the offer was made. Also permitted
withdrawal after 60 days under SEC regulations if the securities  were
being tied up by a purchaser while he decided whether or not to buy them.
Required any person or group acquiring the ownership of more than 10
percent of a company's securities  to disclose similar information.
Authorized the SEC to require a company purchasing its own securities
on the open market to disclose information which the SEC considered in the
public interest. Purchase of shares in a firm by a pension plan for employees
of the firm did not come under the act if the plan were operated
independently of the firm.
Specified that the requirements of the bill applied to securities  registered
under the Securities  Exchange Act of 1934.
Background
The cash tender offer has been used with increasing frequency as a method
to acquire control of publicly held corporations. In a cash tender offer, a
person offers to buy shares of a company, usually at a price above the
current market price. Stockholders who accept the offer tender their 
securities  for purchase, and the person making the offer obligates himself
to buy all or part of the shares. A key advantage of such a strategy over other
means of gaining control, such as a proxy fight, is that the person making the
offer is able to operate in total secrecy, using a bank or a brokerage office as
an agent for buying the shares.
The Senate Banking and Currency Committee reported S 510 (S Rept 550)
Aug. 29, 1967, and the Senate passed the bill by voice vote the next day.

House
Committee Action. The House Committee on Interstate and Foreign
Commerce July 12 reported S 510 (H Rept 1711) with amendments.
The Committee noted in its report that the number of attempts to gain control
of firms listed on stock exchanges through cash tender offers increased to
100 in 1966 from eight in 1960.
“The bill would correct the current gap in our securities  laws (by
providing) for full disclosure  in connection with cash tender offers and
other techniques for accumulating large blocks of equity securities …,” the
Committee said.
The Committee amended the Senate bill to include certain closed-end
investment companies inadvertently left out of the Senate version; to provide
that the name of the bank supplying credit for a tender offer be kept
confidential; to make clear the authority of the Securities and Exchange
Commission under the act; and to make it clear that the purchase of shares
in a firm by a pension plan operated by persons independent of the firm did
not come under the act.
Floor Action. The House July 15 passed S 510 by a voice vote as reported by the Committee.
