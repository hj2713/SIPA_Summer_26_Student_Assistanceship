Interest Rate Controls
An article from CQ Almanac 1967

Background
Congressional Action
Congress Sept. 20 completed action on an Administration-backed bill (S
1956) to extend for one year a 1966 law (PL 89-597) giving flexible authority
to federal bank regulatory agencies to set different limits on the interest
rates that banks and other financial institutions could pay on different types
and amounts of fixed maturity (time) deposits.
The President signed S 1956 into law (PL 90-87) on Sept. 21, the day that
PL 89-597 was to expire. As enacted, S 1956 simply extended the 1966 law
for one year, through Sept. 21, 1968. The Administration and the bank
regulatory agencies involved had recommended an extension of two years or
longer.
As enacted, PL 90-87 continued the current authority of the Federal Reserve
Board (Fed), the Federal Home Loan Bank Board (FHLBB) and the Federal
Deposit Insurance Corp. (FDIC) to set interest rate  ceilings according to
such criteria as geographic location and size of deposits. The three regulatory
agencies used these flexible powers to moderate competition among
institutions for consumer savings. The bill also extended other provisions of
PL 89-597, including one which gave the FHLBB authority for the first time to
establish directly interest  and dividend rates which savings and loan
institutions could pay their depositors.
The bill was enacted with little controversy. A relatively minor issue arose in the House when Banking and Currency
Committee Chairman Wright Patman (D Texas) attempted to add language requiring an annual audit of the Federal
Reserve System. The proposal was defeated in Committee. Patman for many years had been an outspoken critic of
the Federal Reserve System.
Background
REFERENCE – 1966 Almanac p. 753.
Congress in 1966 enacted PL 89-597 to protect savings and loan associations (S & Ls), the traditional source of
financing for home building, from the competition of high- interest  certificates of deposit (CDs) issued by
commercial banks.
The banks in 1966, in order to acquire funds to meet a high demand for industrial and consumer loans, began to
offer the certificates in sizes far smaller than had been offered in the past and at interest  rates higher than S &
Ls could afford to pay. The result was that persons with relatively small amounts of money to invest took their funds
out of S & Ls (or did not go to S & Ls in the first place) and instead bought small denomination CDs from banks.
This in turn limited funds available to S & Ls and dried up the major source of home financing; a sharp downturn in
housing industry activity was one of the most prominent imbalances in the economy during 1966.
The “ rate  war” for savings arose because existing law required federal authorities to set a single general limit
on time deposits, with variations permitted only with respect to differing maturities and other related factors. Thus,
when banks began offering the CDs in low denominations, authorities could reduce their attractiveness only by
lowering the rates on time deposits across the board. Because this would have included large denomination CDs,
an obligation bought in substantial quantities by major corporations, it would have diminished a major source of
funds available to banks for lending.
Another major provision of PL 89-597 gave the FHLBB authority for the first time to establish directly interest
and dividend rates which savings and loan institutions could pay their depositors. This authority applied to general
savings accounts, as opposed to the CDs – that is, time deposits – discussed above. The FHLBB had long sought
to obtain this authority over S & L interest  rates on general savings accounts. S & L “ rate wars” in some

Congressional Action
Senate. S 1956 as introduced extended PL 89-597 for two years, through Sept. 21, 1969. The Senate Banking and
Currency Committee July 13 reported S 1956 (S Rept 396) without amendment. The Senate July 17 passed the bill
by voice vote without amendment.
House. Rep. Wright Patman (D Texas), chairman of the House Banking and Currency Committee, Aug. 31 introduced
a bill (HR 12754) authorizing a two-year extension of PL 89-597 and providing for an annual audit of the Federal
Reserve System by the Government Accounting Office. Patman's Committee held two days of hearings Sept. 14–15
on HR 12754. In an executive session Sept. 19 the Committee voted 17–11 to delete from the bill the Federal
Reserve audit provision and voted unanimously to change the extension of PL 89-597 from two years to one year.
HR 12754 was sent directly to the floor Sept. 19 without a report; this action helped speed final action because the
1966 law was to expire two days later. On the floor, Patman proposed amendments as directed by the Committee;
they were accepted by voice vote. The bill, providing only for a one-year extension of PL 89-597, was passed by
voice vote. The House then substituted the one-year extension in HR 12754 for the two-year extension in S 1956
and passed the amended S 1956 by voice vote.
Final Action. The Senate Sept. 20 accepted the House amendment for a one-year extension and sent the bill to
the President.
