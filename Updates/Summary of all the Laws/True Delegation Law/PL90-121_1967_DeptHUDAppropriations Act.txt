Model Cities, Rent Supplement Funds Cut Deeply

Features
Two of the Johnson Administration's major urban assistance programs – rent supplements and model cities – were given
additional financing by Congress in 1967, but at levels far below those sought by the President.
For new rent supplements contract authority, Congress provided $10 million; President Johnson requested $40 million. For the
model cities program, Congress appropriated $312 million; the President requested $662 million.
In addition to the new contract authority for supplements, Congress provided the full $5 million appropriation sought by the
Administration for payments under existing supplements contracts. Because the Government was committed to the contracts, the
payments were not controversial.
Congress during the 1966 session had approved $32 million for contracts to get the supplements program under way and $11
million to plan the model cities projects (which were called “demonstration cities” in 1966). The full supplements allotment was
committed in 1967, but the Government froze the cities funds pending an appropriation to carry out the projects. The initial $11
million in planning money was committed to 63 cities on Nov. 16. Because some of these cities were represented by House
Members who were instrumental in securing the fiscal 1968 cities appropriations, their inclusion aroused considerable
controversy. (For list of cities, see p. 486.)
Of the new allotment for the cities program, $12 million was for additional project planning and $300 million for carrying out the
plans. The $300 million figure included $100 million for urban renewal in the model cities project areas and $200 million for all the
other programs in the projects.
The funding for the two programs constituted relatively small items in one of the larger appropriations bills enacted during the 1st
Session of the 90th Congress. The bill (HR 9960) covered the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) and
various independent agencies. HR 9960 appropriated a total of $10,139,473,900. The President signed it Nov. 3 (PL 90 - 121
).
The bulk of the funds in the bill, including more than $6.6 billion for veterans' affairs, aroused little or no controversy. But the
items for rent supplements and model cities were at issue throughout the year much as was the case when the programs were
enacted in the 89th Congress. Moreover, the fact that final action on HR 9960 occurred in October in the midst of an
antispending fervor in the House of Representatives made it all the more remarkable that Congress provided as much as it did
for either program. (The bill was cleared Oct. 26.)
The difficulty in obtaining financing for the programs was expected when Congress convened. Both were enacted over vocal
conservative opposition in 1965 and 1966 in a Congress that was much more liberal than the Congress which convened in
January 1967. During the 1966 Congressional election campaign, many Republican candidates had promised to try to kill both
programs.
Throughout the session, the rent supplements allotment was more in doubt than the model cities financing. The cities program
had obtained strong backing from big city mayors, so funding at some level was expected. Rent supplements, however, had
drawn the support of only a handful of special interest groups; moreover, some of these groups did not lobby extensively for the
program, according to observers who followed the action on HR 9960 during the year.
Because the aid provided by both programs would benefit a relatively small number of persons, there was no major grass-roots
effort to persuade Members of Congress to vote for either of the allotments. on the other hand, some Members said they
received considerable mail from residents of suburban areas who opposed the programs.

reportedly made it necessary for the Administration to bargain to obtain the votes of Members from rural and predominantly non-

The reluctance to continue the programs – much less expand them – centered in the House. The more liberal Senate was
basically receptive to increased financing. In actions on the program earlier in the year, the House dropped all the contract
authority for supplements and allotted only $237 million for model cities. The Senate restored the requested $40 million for
supplements and voted $537 million in cities funds. House conferees refused to meet the Senate halfway, however, and even the
$10 million compromise on supplements cleared the House by only 14 votes. (See details below.)
In view of the mood of the House, President Johnson Nov. 3 called the appropriations “a legislative miracle.” The President
praised the Senate for restoring much of the House cuts but said that in the House-Senate conference, the “familiar old voices of
reaction and status quo” had prevailed.
Despite the cuts in the cities allotment, H. Ralph Taylor, the HUD Assistant Secretary in charge of the model cities program, said
cities funds still would go to an estimated 60 to 70 urban areas – the same number contemplated before the funds were cut.
Rather than reduce the number of participants, he indicated, HUD preferred to cut the participants' shares. A total of 191 cities
and counties had applied for program aid.
Other Issues. In another major action on the bill, Congress handed the Administration a clear-cut victory by approving the
President's full request to sell participation certificates (PCs) totaling $3,235,000,000 in pools of Government loans and assets.
(Of the total, $2,385,000,000 were in HUD securities and $850 million were in Veterans Administration securities.) The House
had pared the total allotment to $881 million, but the Senate restored the full request and conferees upheld the Senate position.
In addition, the bill appropriated funds for payments of insufficiencies in connection with the participation sales. The insufficiencies
were the difference between the interest rate paid to the Government on the securities which were pooled for participation sales
to the public and the higher interest rate the Government probably would have to pay to make the participations attractive to
buyers. For insufficiencies on HUD PCs, the bill appropriated $23 million; for insufficiencies on the VA participations, it
appropriated $665,000.
Another controversy on HR 9960 developed when the Senate Appropriations Committee emphasized in its report on the bill that
projects constructed for housing the recipients of rent supplements normally should be financed in the private market and in
cases where nonprofit sponsors (such as labor unions and church groups) found it necessary to seek Government financing, they
should put up an equity investment of at least 5 percent. Proponents of supplements feared this would kill the program, but their
concern was abated when the insurance industry pledged a $1 billion commitment to loans for low-income housing. Officials of
the industry said most of the funds would be committed to the financing of rent supplements projects.
Ironically, the largest item in the bill never was put to debate. This was a request of $6,651,014,000 for the Veterans'
Administration, of which all but $1,735,000 was approved in the final bill.
The total appropriations provided by HR 9960 were $681,039,800 less than Administration requests but $559,383,600 more than
the amounts appropriated for the same purposes in two appropriations bills for fiscal 1967 – the Independent Offices
Appropriations Act (HR 14921) and the Second Supplemental Appropriations bill (HR 9481). The final allotment in HR 9960 was
$375,357,000 less than the amount voted by the Senate and $126,295,118 more than the sum approved by the House.
Funds for numerous activities normally funded in the annual independent offices money bill were not carried in HR 9960.
Appropriations for the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) and the Appalachian Regional Commission were
not included because neither authorization was approved until work was well under way on HR 9960. Funds previously contained
in the bill for the Federal Aviation Agency and certain services of the Civil Aeronautics Board and the Interstate Commerce
Commission had been shifted to the appropriations legislation for the new Cabinet-level Department of Transportation.

House
COMMITTEE – House Appropriations.
ACTION – May 12 reported HR 9960 (H Rept 259) recommending $10,032,905,900 – a cut of $771,736,800 from the
Administration request. The Committee in its report said it had “carefully evaluated over-all requirements” and was recommending
what it considered “reasonable” amounts in light of the Viet Nam war and the impending Budget deficit. The total cut, it said,
represented a 7.1-percent saving from the Administration's Budget request.
Of the committee cuts in Administration requests, all but $83.1 million came from programs administered by the Department of
Housing and Urban Development (HUD). Large reductions were made in the controversial model cities and rent supplements
programs and the entire appropriation was knocked out for a third controversial plan – bonus grants for comprehensive
metropolitan planning.
Following Committee approval of the bill, Rep. Joe L. Evins (D Tenn.), chairman of the Independent Offices-HUD Appropriations
Subcommittee, the panel with original jurisdiction over HUD requests, told newsmen the fact that model cities and rent
supplements were kept alive at all was “a great victory for the Administration.” Evins said the two programs had been put to
“some mighty close votes” in the Subcommittee before funds had been approved. (The Subcommittee allotments were adopted
without change by the full Committee. For amounts, see below; for factors relating to Subcommittee approval, see p. 479.)
Dept. of Housing and Urban Development
Recommended $1,872,765,000 for HUD, a reduction of $688,626,000 from requests. Despite the large cuts, the report
emphasized that the allotment was $386,465,000 above the appropriation granted the Department in the previous fiscal year.
Although the bulk of the increase went for initial funding of programs authorized by the Demonstration Cities and Metropolitan
Development Act of 1966 (PL 89-754), many of the older urban programs received substantial increases over their fiscal 1967
appropriations.
Model Cities. Recommended a total of $237 million for all model cities activities, a reduction of $425 million from requests. While
the Committee approved the full $12 million requested to plan model city projects, it deleted $250 million of the $400 million
requested for actual project grants and $175 million of the $250 million sought for urban renewal programs in project areas. In
justifying its action, the Committee said it saw no need for the full allotment because testimony from mayors and other officials
had indicated it would take six months to a year to prepare and process “specific and adequate plans.”
Rent Supplements. Cut the $40 million request for authority to enter into rent supplement contracts to $10 million but provided
the full $5 million requested for payments under supplements contracts authorized in each of the two previous fiscal years. The
Committee in its report also stipulated that projects constructed for housing the recipients of supplements should be financed
without commitments from the Federal National Mortgage Assn. (FNMA) to buy the mortgages with FNMA special assistance
funds. (Such a commitment by FNMA made it more likely that banks and other lenders would make loans for the supplements
projects, because the FNMA purchase would assure them new funds to make additional loans.) The Committee said that if the
state of the private mortgage market made private financing unlikely or impractical, “then the program should be deferred until the
mortgage market warrants.”
Alaska Housing. Denied the full $1 million requested for initial financing of a program of housing grants and loans to the state of
Alaska, as authorized under the 1966 Demonstration Cities Act.
Neighborhood Facilities. Provided $27 million of the $42 million requested for grants for neighborhood health and recreation
centers and other neighborhood facilities, as authorized under the 1965 Housing and Urban Development Act (PL 89-117). (1965
Almanac p. 363)
Urban Renewal. Approved the entire $750 million requested for advance funding of the fiscal 1969 urban renewal program. The
Committee's action continued the practice of financing the renewal program through annual appropriations rather than the
contract authority financing method (also known as back-door financing) which was used for many years; under the old method,
Congress provided funds for urban renewal after projects were already completed. Congress first shifted to the annual
appropriations method in the fiscal 1966 Independent Offices Appropriations Act and endorsed the method again in the fiscal
1967 Act. (1965 Almanac p. 154; 1966 Almanac p. 168)
Public Housing. Approved $275 million of a $290 million request for the annual federal contribution to support public housing
programs.
Housing for the Elderly. Cut to $20 million the Administration's request for an increase of $80 million in the capital for the

Urban Research. Cut $15 million of the $20 million requested for research activities in the area of housing, metropolitan growth
and urban problems. The report noted that the $5 million allotment provided was a tenfold increase over the funds appropriated
in fiscal 1967. It said additional research funds also were available in other HUD programs such as urban renewal, mass
transportation and public housing.
Housing Demonstration Programs. Recommended $2 million of the $2.5 million Budget estimate for low-income housing
demonstration projects, authorized under the 1961 Housing Act.
Metropolitan Expediters. Denied the full $1,580,000 requested to provide for appointment of 150 “metropolitan expediters” to
assist local governments and act as liaison between the Federal Government and the cities. The Committee also wrote language
into the bill prohibiting a diversion of any other funds in the Act for use in providing for expediters. The report said the expediters'
proposed functions could be handled adequately by regional offices of HUD and by the staffs of city governments. Expediters
were authorized under the 1966 Demonstration Cities Act.
Participation Sales Authorization. Approved the Administration's request for authority to sell participations in $2,385,000,000 of
HUD's holdings of mortgage paper and other loan assets. The Committee also provided the full $42,115,000 sought for the
probable interest rate differential on the HUD sales.
Other Agencies and Programs
Disaster Relief. Recommended the full Budget estimate of $15 million for the President's emergency disaster relief fund, as
authorized under PL 81-875.
General Services Administration. Approved $548,-913,900 for the GSA, a cut of $19,065,800 from requests. (In addition, the
GSA was granted $27.3 million of a $27.4 million request for indefinite appropriations to cover operating expenses of the
agency's property management and disposal service.) Included in the appropriation were funds to support construction of eight

National Science Foundation. Recommended $495 million of the $526 million requested for NSF – the major Governmental

atmospheric sciences, and oceanography. For one of the four areas – oceanography – the Committee noted that it had provided
the full Budget request.
Selective Service System. Approved the full Budget estimate of $57,455,000 for the Selective Service System. Said the
estimate was based on the induction of 285,000 registrants as compared with an estimate of 311,000 for the previous fiscal year.
Veterans Administration. Recommended $6,648,035,000 for the VA, the largest item in the bill. Cuts were made in only three
items: $1.1 million in general operating expenses, $650,000 for medical administration and $1.2 million for medical research. The
Committee in a related action gave approval to the request for authority to sell participations in $850 million of VA securities and
for $946,000 in appropriations to cover the probable interest rate differential.
Civil Defense. Recommended $86.1 million of a $111 million request for civil defense programs.

Floor Action
The House May 17, by a 347–56 roll-call vote, passed an amended version of HR 9960, appropriating $10,013,-178,782. (See
vote 56, p. 26-H.)
In a major defeat for the Administration, the House by a 233–171 roll-call vote, accepted an amendment by Glenn R. Davis (R
Wis.) deleting the $10 million in rent supplement contract authority contained in the Committee version of the bill. (The
amendment did not, however, affect the $5 million in rent supplement payments that had been approved by the Committee to
honor existing supplements contracts.) (See vote 54, p. 26-H.)
The Administration won a partial victory when the House, by a 193–213 roll-call vote, rejected a recommittal motion by Robert H.
Michel (R Ill.) to send the bill back to the Appropriations Committee with instructions to delete all model cities funds except the
$12 million allotted for planning. An identical amendment by Michel had been rejected earlier on a 152–160 teller vote. (For
breakdown on roll-call, see vote 55, p. 26-H.)
A factor apparently affecting the vote on model cities was the vigorous lobbying effort staged in behalf of the program by big city
mayors and other urban interests. Rent supplements, by contrast, did not have a strong lobby.
Deletion of the contract authority for rent supplements was the first floor defeat that President Johnson's “Great Society” program
had suffered during the 1967 session.
The vote on rent supplements also was the first occasion of the session on which the conservative coalition – a voting alliance of
Republicans and Southern Democrats against Northern Democrats – had joined together to defeat a major Administration
proposal (R 163–12, SD 59–27, ND 11–132). The only Northern Democrats favoring the amendment were Baring (Nev.), Fallen
(Md.), Garmatz (Md.), Hamilton (Ind.), Ichord (Mo.), Jones (Mo.), McCarthy (N.Y.), Pike (N.Y.), Randall (Mo.), Stratton (N.Y.) and
Tunney (Calif.). The only Republicans opposing it were Brown (Mich.), Button (N.Y.), Gude (Md.), Halpern (N.Y.)
Harvey (Mich.), Horton (N.Y.), Kupferman (N.Y.), Mathias (Md.), Morse (Mass.), Mosher (Ohio), Reid (N.Y.) and Whalen (Ohio).
Four of the GOP Members voting “no” – Brown, Button, Gude and Whalen – were freshmen.)
The coalition formed again for the recommittal vote on model cities but was unable to hold the level of support it had commanded
on rent supplements and was defeated (R 141–35; SD 47–39; ND 5–139). (For analysis of factors resulting in the outcome of
both the supplements and model cities votes, see box p. 478.)
Other Amendments. In other action on HR 9960, the House by nonrecord votes accepted GOP-sponsored amendments to
drastically reduce the Administration's proposed authority to make participation sales. Total authority for the sales was cut from
$3,235,000,000 to $881 million, including cuts in the HUD authority from $2,385,000,000 to $581 million and in VA authority from
$850 million to $300 million. Republicans argued that the Administration was using the sales as a device to reduce the size of the
Budget deficit. The funds left in the bill were to cover new loans under the programs, Republicans said.
In one other major action, the House accepted an amendment by William C. Cramer (R Fla.) to prohibit use of any funds in the bill
to implement a provision of the 1966 Demonstration Cities and Urban Development Act requiring review by an areawide planning
agency of most federal aid programs submitted from metropolitan areas. Cramer argued that the provision, which was to take
effect July 1, 1967, would mean a cutoff of aid for areas where there was no constitutional authority to appoint the planning body.
He said the purpose of his amendment was to ensure that the requirement would not be implemented. (1966 Almanac p. 227.)
In debate on the model cities and rent supplements plans, little was added to the point made by supporters and opponents in
previous debates on the programs. Opponents argued that both programs were “socialistic” and that it would be indefensible to
fund them in light of requirements for the Viet Nam war. Supporters retorted that the “festering” problems of the cities should
receive top budgetary priority despite the needs of the war. (See box on “pros and cons” of the two programs, p. 474.)

COMMITTEE – Senate Appropriations.

The Committee in its report said it was a “misconception to believe that the amount of the participation sales authorization should
be determined solely by the amount required to finance the 1968 program level of the agencies whose assets are being placed in
the pool, or that participation sales in 1968 should equal loan commitments in each program.” In order to remove a burden from
the Treasury (which financed the original loans), the report said, private financing (achieved by making the participation sales)
would be preferable whenever the market could absorb the participations at reasonable interest rates. In this manner, the report
said, the Government would return to its “historic” function of acting as “an intermediary, not as a banker.”

Floor Action
The Senate Sept. 21, by a 77–5 roll-call vote, passed an amended version of HR 9960, appropriating $10,514–830,900. ( See
vote 200, p. 46-S.)
In the major floor developments on the bill, the Senate Sept. 20 voted to sustain the Committee's restoration of the full
Administration request for rent supplement contract authority and its partial restoration of model cities funds. The Senate also
sustained all the other major Committee changes, including the restoration of the full Administration request for participation sales
authority and additional funds for an array of key urban programs.
In addition to its actions on the Committee amendments, the Senate accepted six and rejected four other floor amendments. The
amendments approved included four minor and two major proposals – amendments to increase the Committee's allotment for the
National Science Foundation by $46 million, and the President's disaster relief fund by $10 million. The amendments rejected
included a proposal to drop half of the $40 million rent supplements allotment, cut $20 million from the Committee's $91.1 million
allowance for civil defense, and two separate efforts to reduce appropriations for new public building construction. The first of the
proposals to cut the buildings allotment envisioned reducing the allowance to the original Administration request, while the second
was designed to eliminate the new building projects in Mobile, Seattle, and Washington, D.C.
Economy Issue. In a long colloquy just before passage, John J. Williams (R Del.) criticized Administration spending policies and
said that many of the funds in the bill had been requested just so the President could announce a large reduction in federal
expenditures. The purpose of this “propaganda maneuver,” Williams said, was to “build up the idea of a great economy
movement” and thus gain support for the President's proposed income tax increase.
In answer to Williams' charges, Warren G. Magnuson (D Wash.), the bill's floor manager, said all the funds in the measure were for
priority domestic objectives and that the President would find it difficult to defer any of them. Despite the Viet Nam war,
Magnuson indicated, the fiscal 1968 budget deficit might not be much greater than the $12.4 billion deficit recorded by the
Republican Administration of President Eisenhower in fiscal 1959. Terming the Eisenhower years “eight years of drought,”
Magnuson said there had been “no program for schools…no program for cities” and little other domestic social legislation.
Magnuson's assertions were disputed by Thruston B. Morton (R Ky.), who was Republican National Chairman from 1959–61. He
said that “if President Eisenhower were 60 years old today instead of 76, we would have a wonderful candidate for 1968.” Amid
laughter, Magnuson said he would amend his remarks “to say that comparatively nothing was done.”
The five Senators voting against the bill were Williams, Bourke B. Hickenlooper (R Iowa), Ernest F. Hollings (D S.C.), Jack Miller (R
Iowa), and Strom Thurmond (R S.C.). Like Williams, the other dissenters contended that the bill provided unnecessary
appropriations.
Amendments
Rent Supplements. In one of the most important Administration victories of the session, the Senate Sept. 20 voted
overwhelming approval of the $40 million supplements allotment. After a long debate on a side issue (see below), the Senate by
a 34–55 roll-call vote rejected an amendment by Frank J. Lausche (R Ohio) to reduce the allowance to $20 million. It then
approved the Committee amendment by a roll call of 59–31. (See votes 195–196, p. 45-S.)
A key factor in the outcome on supplements was the shift to support of the program by Senate Minority Leader Everett McKinley
Dirksen (R Ill.), who previously had opposed it. Dirksen Aug. 8 announced his switch after learning that numerous business
leaders had endorsed the program at a recent housing conference in Pittsburgh. In his Aug. 8 statement, Dirksen said: “I now
think I understand the program a little better. Now we may be able to get the Government out of public housing.” Dirksen made
no further comment on the issue on the Senate floor but voted against the Lausche proposal and for the Committee amendment.
Most of the debate on the supplements issue skirted the merits of the program itself and focused on the Committee's
recommendation that the nonprofit sponsors using FNMA special assistance be required to put up 5 percent of project costs.
Because the recommendation was not part of the bill, it could not be deleted or modified by a floor amendment. Also, since
Senate rules prohibited the inclusion of legislation in an appropriations bill, it was impossible for opponents of the Committee
language to write countervailing instructions into the bill. Consequently, numerous Senators spoke against the recommendation in
hopes that housing officials would interpret their advice as more representative of the “sense of the Senate” than was that of the
Committee report.

Another strong opponent of the proviso was John J. Sparkman (D Ala.), chairman of the Banking and Currency Committee and of

come from church-affiliated groups. If the language of the report were followed, he contended, more than half these organizations

The leading proponent of the Committee recommendation. Allen J. Ellender (D La.), a member of the HUD-Independent Offices
Appropriations Subcommittee, said the requirement was necessary because “anyone interested in providing for housing should
have some kind of investment in it.” If the nonprofit groups put up an equity, he said, they would be less likely to abandon the
projects in the event they ran into financial difficulty. In addition, Ellender said, the nonprofit groups should have little trouble
raising the 5 percent because the figure was not limited to cash alone but could also include the land on which the project was
constructed, the building materials, labor, “or anything of value that would contribute to construction….”
Joining Ellender in cautioning HUD against ignoring the recommendation were HUD Appropriations Subcommittee Chairman
Warren G. Magnuson (D Wash.), ranking Subcommittee Republican Gordon Allott (Colo.) and Spessard L. Holland (D Fla.), another
member of the panel. Magnuson, emphasizing that he personally opposed the recommendation, said it had been necessary for
him to accept it in order to obtain the Subcommittee votes for approval of the rent supplement funds. If the Department ignored
the proviso, he indicated, it might endanger future HUD appropriations.
Model Cities. The Senate Sept. 20, by a 62–28 roll call, accepted the Committee amendment restoring all but $125 million of the
House cut for model cities. (See vote 194, p. 45-S.)
At the outset of debate on the issue, Ribicoff, whose Government Operations Executive Reorganization Subcommittee had
conducted lengthy hearings on urban problems in 1966 and 1967, said refusal to fund the program adequately would be a vote to
allow U.S. cities “to sink deeper and deeper into degradation and violence.” Contending that recent outbreaks of urban rioting
were caused by “apathy, hopelessness and sheer misery,” Ribicoff said the “cities” plan would be a “large and important step” to
counter these problems. Noting that cities in all but three states had applied for the program, Ribicoff said the funds would aid all
sections of the nation.
Opponents of the program, led by Allott, said that only effective law enforcement and not additional urban aid could stop the riots.
Allott said it was “obvious that the fundamental purpose” of the rioters was not social reform but to “create disorder and destroy
property and lives.” Allott also said that because no cities yet had been selected to begin planning the projects and because
planning would take about 6 to 12 months, the House allotment would be adequate for the present fiscal year. Magnuson
retorted, however, that since the “cities” plan was a two-year program, all the funds eventually would be needed once the
program got underway. “We do not want to cut (the funds) off at midnight on June 30, 1968.” he asserted, “and then start a new
program all over again.”
FHA Jurisdiction Issue. The Senate averted another major controversy when Edward W. Brooke (R Mass.) agreed Sept. 20 to
withdraw a pending floor amendment to transfer $1,150,000 in funds to administer the rent supplements program from the
Federal Housing Administration to the Office of the HUD Secretary.
Brooke, expressing dissatisfaction over FHA's administration of the Government's low-income housing programs, said he had
introduced the amendment as a “first and necessary step” in a long-range package of proposals that he would sponsor to
reconstitute all FHA low-income programs in a new division of low-income housing within HUD. Because of FHA delays in
processing applications for sponsorship of rent supplements projects, Brooke said, only 38 new units actually had been
constructed and only an additional 104 units were in or near the construction stage. (For story on the issue, see p. 488.)
After several other Senators had expressed support for his amendment, Brooke agreed to withdraw it when Banking and
Currency Committee Chairman John J. Sparkman promised to hold hearings on the matter as soon as the panel had completed
action on home ownership bills and other major urban aid proposals. Sparkman earlier had threatened to raise a point of order
against the amendment on grounds that it was legislation on an appropriations bill.
Participation Sales. The Senate Sept. 21, by a 57–30 roll-call vote, sustained the Committee amendment restoring the full
$2,385,000,000 request for HUD participation sales. In an earlier action Sept. 19, it sustained the Committee's restoration of the
full $850 million allotment in VA participation sales authority. (See vote 192, p. 45-S; vote 199, p. 46-S.)
In debate on the issue, John J. Williams (R Del.), the principal opponent of the sales, argued that the “sole purpose of…this
procedure to finance the cost of Government is to give the Administration a chance to deceive the American taxpayers as to the
true amount” of the budget deficit. To float the sales, he asserted, the Government would have to pay an average interest rate
0.6 percent higher than the interest income of the securities to be placed in the pool.
Allott, another opponent of the sales, argued that the participations discriminated against small investors because the smallest
denomination in which participations could be purchased was $5,000. “What we are doing by this process,” he said, “is giving to
the people who manage large funds, loan institutions, mortgage institutions, insurance institutions, foundations, people who can
invest in large amounts, in effect a one-half percent interest subsidy over what they could get on a U.S. bond.”

Conference
Initial Agreement
House and Senate conferees Oct. 20 filed a conference report (H Rept 803) resolving all differences between the two bills except
those on rent supplements and model cities. The conference committee reported both these provisions “in disagreement,” which
meant that conferees would seek instructions from their respective chambers on how to resolve the two issues.
Following are the major agreements reached by conferees in their Oct. 20 report:
Department of Housing and Urban Development. Provided a compromise figure of $1,636,000,000 for HUD activities. (This
figure included all HUD items except model cities funds. The rent supplements item still in dispute was not an appropriation but a
contract authorization.) The conference allotment was $263,391,000 less than the Administration request for these activities,
$116,148,000 less than the Senate amount and $19,350,000 more than the House sum.
On major specific HUD items, conferees:
Alaska Housing. Sustained the House position dropping the full $1 million request (Senate: $1 million) for housing grants and
loans for the state of Alaska.
Neighborhood Facilities. Approved an allotment of $30 million (request and Senate: $42 million; House: $27 million) for
neighborhood facilities grants.
Housing for the Elderly. Agreed to an appropriation of $25 million (request: $80 million; House: $20 million; Senate: $40 million)
to replenish the revolving fund for direct housing loans for the elderly and handicapped.
Urban Planning Grants. Provided a compromise $45 million (request and Senate: $50 million; House: $40 million) for urban
planning grants.
Open-Space – Urban Beautification. Agreed to the House figure of $75 million (request: $125 million; Senate: $100 million) for
open-space land grants. Also accepted House language limiting the federal share of any project to a maximum of 50 percent.
Mass Transportation. Adopted the House figure of $175 million (request: $230 million; Senate: $205 million) for urban mass
transit grants.
Community Training. Agreed to an appropriation of $3 million (request: $5 million; House: $2.5 million; Senate: $4.5 million) for
community development training grants. Conferees in a related action sustained the request and Senate figure of $500,000
(House: none) for college fellowships in city planning and urban studies.
Urban Research. Approved a compromise allowance of $10 million (request: $20 million; House: $5 million; Senate: $15 million)
for urban research and urban technology studies.
Metropolitan Expediters, Areawide Planning Limitation. Agreed to House provisos, dropped by the Senate, which prohibited
use of any part of the bill's appropriations to provide “metropolitan expediters” for federal-local liaison, or to administer an
areawide planning requirement of the 1966 Demonstration Cities Act. (See box p. 473.)
Participation Sales. Sustained the Senate's restoration of the full $2,385,000,000 authorization sought by the Administration
(House: $581 million) for HUD participation sales. Conferees in another action, however, agreed to the House allowance of $23
million (request and Senate: $42,115,000) to make up the possible differential between the interest rates paid the Government on
the pooled assets and the rates necessary to market the participations. (Sources told Congressional Quarterly they doubted the
lower appropriation for the rate differential would deter the Government from marketing the full authorization. Because the
participations were backed by the “full faith” of the Government, they said, Congress would find it difficult to deny additional
interest subsidy funds if they were needed.)

Report Adopted
The first conference report was adopted by voice votes of both the House and Senate on Oct. 24. After approving the report,
however, each chamber voted to reject the allotments provided by the other for rent supplements and model cities. A new
conference then was called to attempt to resolve the two remaining disputes.
Senate rejection of the two House figures (nothing for supplements and $237 million for model cities) came by voice vote. House
rejection of the Senate allotments ($40 million for supplements and $537 million for cities) came on a 156–241 roll-call vote on
the cities issue (R 15–159; ND 125–18; SD 16–64) and a 151–251 roll call on supplements (R 15–163; ND 120–24; SD 16–64).
The “conservative coalition” formed in opposition to the Senate figures on both House votes. (See votes 176, 177, p. 80-H.)
Prior to House adoption of the conference report, another controversy developed when Republicans sought to recommit the
report to conference with instructions to House conferees to reject the Senate figures for participation sales. Following a heated
debate, the GOP motion lost by a 184–208 roll-call vote. (See vote 175, p. 80-H.)
In debate on the participation sales issue, Charles Raper Jonas (R N.C.), the leading House opponent of the participations, argued
that the total cost of the interest subsidies to float the sales could amount to as much as $700 million over the next 10 years.
William B. Widnall (R N.J.), ranking GOP member of the Banking and Currency Committee, added that the high yields to be paid
on the participations would draw funds from savings and loan institutions – the principal source of home mortgage loans – and
“provoke a new crisis” in the housing market. House Ways and Means Committee Chairman Wilbur D. Mills (D Ark.) defended the
sales, arguing that the Government borrowing that would become necessary if the sales authority were rejected might push
interest rates even higher than the level the Government might have to pay to float the sales.
In the vote on the participations, Republicans unanimously opposed the Senate figure (172–0) but were able to command the
votes of only 12 Democrats. All but eight Southern Democrats voted with a majority of Northern Democrats against the GOP
move. This was in contrast to numerous other votes on economy issues (both on HR 9960 and on other bills) when the
“conservative coalition” was able to defeat Administration requests.
Second Conference Agreement, Final Action
The new House-Senate conference Oct. 25 agreed unanimously to a second conference report (H Rept 822) providing
compromise allotments of $10 million for rent supplement contracts and $312 million in appropriations for model cities. (The new
cities allotment included $12 million for planning, $100 million for urban renewal and $200 million for other programs in model
cities projects.)
The report was adopted Oct. 26 by voice vote of the Senate and by a 297–88 roll-call vote of the House. Although the House
GOP conferees supported the compromise agreements, rank and file House Republicans moved to recommit the report with
instructions to drop the rent supplements funds, but their motion was rejected by a roll call of 184–198 (R 139–35; ND 6–124;
SD 39–39). House and Senate adoption of the second report cleared the bill for the President. (See votes 182, 183, p. 82-H.)

Areawide Planning Controversy
Congress in the final version of HR 9960 approved a House floor amendment designed to block the Department of Housing and
Urban Development (HUD) from implementing a controversial provision of the 1966 housing legislation which required local
officials within standard metropolitan areas to submit applications for most kinds of federal aid to an areawide planning agency for
review.
The amendment, sponsored by William C. Cramer (R Fla.), specifically prohibited HUD from using any part of the bill's funds to
administer or implement the requirement. The requirement – Section 204 of the 1966 Demonstration Cities Act – took effect July
1, 1967.
Cramer said his amendment was necessary because HUD might use the provision to gain more control over local planning
decisions. Because of a huge backlog of aid applications, he argued, the areawide agencies would tend to make the kind of
recommendations that would be favored by HUD. If they did not, he contended, the applications probably would go to the bottom
of the stack.
Cramer during the Oct. 24 debate on the conference report on HR 9960 said that HUD, following House approval of his
amendment in May, had transferred authority over the requirement to the Bureau of the Budget, Since that time, he said, the
Bureau had implemented the provision in an orderly manner without prejudice to any local area (including those that lagged in
forming an areawide agency). In the meantime, he said, Governors of the states in which metropolitan areas did not have the
areawide agencies had designated similar bodies to perform the required review.

Model Cities and Rent Supplements Programs: Major Urban Assistance Embroiled in Controversy
Following is the background of the model cities and rent supplements programs together with arguments for and against them.
RENT SUPPLEMENTS
REFERENCE – 1966 Almanac p. 245; 1965 Almanac p. 361.
The rent supplements program – a key “Great Society” plan to improve housing conditions for low-income families – was
authorized by Title I of the Housing and Urban Development Act of 1965 (PL 89-117) after barely surviving strong attacks.
As approved by Congress, the supplements plan authorized the Government to contract with local housing sponsors (which were
to be either nonprofit service groups or limited-dividend or cooperative housing organizations) to pay the difference between 25
percent of a poor person's income and his rent in standard housing. The sponsors were to reserve part of their housing space to
accommodate subsidized tenants, and the Government was to contract to meet the necessary subsidies on that space for
periods of up to 40 years. The bill authorized $30 million for supplements in fiscal 1966, to be increased by $35 million in fiscal
1967, $40 million in fiscal 1968 and $45 million in fiscal 1969.
Authorized by narrow margins over almost unanimous GOP opposition, the supplements plan was denied funds at the close of
the 1965 Congressional session when critics contended the Administration's guidelines for program eligibility overstepped the
bounds established in the statute. Although the guidelines later were revised substantially, the supplements program remained
one of the most controversial issues before the 89th Congress.
After continued bitter debate, Congress in 1966 finally provided funds for the program but not before it had cut deeply into
Administration reques'ts. of a total contract allotment of $65 million sought during the session, Congress in two appropriations
bills provided $32 million – $12 million in contracts for fiscal 1966 (PL 89-426) and $20 million for fiscal 1967 (PL 89-555). In
addition, it added to both appropriations measures a restrictive rider granting local officials veto power over supplements projects.
Opponents of the rider feared that local authorities throughout the country might use the veto to block projects they feared would
promote racial or economic integration.
Despite the cut in requests and inclusion of the local control rider, supplements funds, like the original authorization, were
sustained by the barest of margins. The House approved the two appropriations by margins of eight, then four votes. The Senate
vote margin was only one Member on the fiscal 1966 allotment, then 13 Members on the fiscal 1967 allowance.
Arguments for Rent Supplements
The major argument in favor of rent supplements was that the public housing program alone had not been able to accommodate
a large enough portion of the nation's poor and that the rent supplements concept was vastly superior to public housing.
Because eligibility for public housing was limited to persons of low income, the proponents of supplements argued that the public
housing project has essentially the same atmosphere as an urban ghetto. In many if not all cities, they said, most public housing
occupants were members of racial or ethnic minorities.
On the other hand, the supplements supporters contended, the fact that the supplements plan placed subsidized tenants in
standard housing with nonsubsidized, higher-income families tends to give the lower-income occupant an incentive to increase
his income. This contrasts with the situation in public housing, where a tenant has to give up his dwelling when his income
exceeds program eligibility levels. When the supplements tenant's income rises above the local eligibility ceiling, he ceases to
receive the supplement but may remain in the project and pay the full rent.
Another key argument in favor of rent supplements was that the role of private enterprise in sponsoring the projects provides a
distinct advantage over local-government sponsorship of public housing. Because the private groups sponsoring supplements
projects pay for construction and administration, proponents argued, the supplements program costs the taxpayer less. An
additional advantage, they said, is that because private enterprise owns the supplements housing projects they are fully taxable
to the locality, whereas public housing is tax exempt.
Arguments Against Rent Supplements
Opponents of supplements characterized the program as a “socialistic giveaway.” They contended it was particularly indefensible
to fund the plan at a time of mounting budgetary pressures for the Viet Nam war.
Perhaps the most important factor working against supplements was the unpopularity with numerous Members of Congress of the
basic concept of taxing one person to pay another's rent. Following House rejection of supplements funds May 17, one House
GOP source told CQ that “the name of the program alone was enough to kill it. It's easy to get people riled up over a program
that advertises itself as a subsidy.” (A high Administration official, who later left the Government, told CQ in 1965 that it had been
a “drastic mistake” to label the program “rent supplements.” He said he had proposed that the plan be “slipped into the (1965)
housing bill as an innocuous program to broaden public housing” but that he had been overruled by the White House, which
sought to make the concept a “cornerstone of the Great Society.”
Another argument of opponents was that the supplements program was potentially too costly because of its 40-year contract
authority. A $10-million contract authorization really amounts to $10 million for 40 years, or a total commitment of $400 million.
(The actual Government expenditure, however, probably would be less because Government payments under the contracts would

MODEL CITIES
REFERENCE – 1966 Almanac p. 210.
The model cities program, known at the time of its enactment as “demonstration cities,” was authorized in the Demonstration
Cities and Metropolitan Development Act of 1966 (PL 89-754). Housing officials late in 1966 renamed the program because they
feared that the racial and antiwar demonstrations that had taken place in many cities during the year had made the original name
politically vulnerable.
As important a part of the “Great Society” as was the supplements plan, the model cities program authorized additional federal
grants to urban areas for a coordinated attack on blight, an attack aimed at social problems as well as inadequate housing.
Participating cities, to be selected under rigid federal standards, were to receive federal grants of up to 80 percent of the financial
contribution they normally would be required to make (under existing law) as their share of all federally assisted programs in an
approved demonstrations plan. They then would spend the funds first to finance any new or existing program in the
demonstrations plan which did not receive other federal aid. Second, if remaining funds were available, they would be used to
help pay costs of the local contribution required under the federally assisted program.
In a departure from the approach of the existing urban renewal program, which emphasized new construction, the model cities
program envisioned a restructuring of the “total environment” of the “demonstration” neighborhoods. By authorizing additional
federal funds which could be used for education, antipoverty and similar social programs, the bill sought to attack the human
problems as well as the physical factors causing blight and decay.
To fund the cities plan, the bill authorized federal grants to $24 million for planning the projects in fiscal 1967 and fiscal 1968,
and an additional $400 million in fiscal 1968 and $500 million in fiscal 1969 for carrying out the projects. An additional $250
million in urban renewal funds were earmarked for demonstration projects initiated in and after fiscal 1968. Aid to an estimated 60
to 70 cities was contemplated under the bill. Other cities were expected' to benefit from observing the example of good
coordination which the bill's supporters hoped would be apparent in the “model cities.”
Although the 1966 Act provided essentially the level of annual funding for demonstrations requested by the President, it did not
provide the long-range program Mr. Johnson had sought. His original request envisioned a $2.3 billion program with one year of
planning and five years of implementation (fiscal 1967–72). He subsequently requested an additional $600 million for urban
renewal activities in the demonstration cities. An intensive Administration lobbying effort salvaged the original program in the
House Banking and Currency Committee's Housing Subcommittee, but the Administration later was forced to compromise when
considerable opposition arose in the Senate. Despite the dilutions, the bill came under a strong floor assault by Republicans in
both House and Senate, and its outcome was never a certainty in either chamber.
Congress at the close of the 1966 session appropriated $11 million in fiscal 1967 funds to pay for planning the demonstration
projects authorized by the bill. This was a cut of only $1 million from the Administration's request. The planning funds had never
been controversial and had enjoyed the support of even the stanchest GOP opponents of the full model city authorization.
Arguments for Model Cities
The major argument in favor of the model cities program was that the nation's urban areas were desperately in need of a
massive attack on blight and that this could be accomplished only through the comprehensive and coordinated federal and local
effort contemplated under the cities plan. In addition, proponents argued, the needed therapy could not be accomplished without
treating the sociological factors causing decay. The emphasis of traditional urban renewal on removing the physical factors simply
was not enough, they said.
Arguments Against Model Cities
Opponents of the cities program contended that the plan could have a substantial effect only if far more federal funds were
committed than the level currently authorized. Consequently, they charged, once the program got started it would “mushroom”
into a multi-billion-dollar commitment.
Another criticism was that the infusion of federal money for the plan would merely induce the cities to make improvements which
they should have undertaken long before with their own resources. Still another issue was that the limited funds authorized for
the program meant that relatively few U.S. cities would get the money to participate.
Conservative Coalition
The once potent conservative coalition in the House, which was weakened in the 89th Congress by the influx of liberal
Democratic Representatives, won a major victory May 17 when it formed to kill funds for the rent supplements program. (The
coalition formed again in subsequent votes on the final bill. See p. 484.)
The term “conservative coalition,” as used by CQ in its voting studies, means a voting alliance of Republicans and Southern
Democrats against Northern Democrats in Congress. Throughout the years since New Deal days, the coalition has been a
powerful force in defeating social welfare legislation. (For over-all coalition performance in 1967, see table of contents.)

Veterans' Benefits
Although the House debated at length and eventually cut from HR 9960 the $10 million recommended by the Appropriations
Committee for rent supplements, there was virtually no debate on the largest item in the bill – $6.6 billion for the Veterans
Administration.
The Committee report on HR 9960 estimated that the benefits provided with these funds would go to 93.5 million people, or 48
percent of the U.S. population – 25.6 million veterans, 64.9 million family members of living veterans and 3 million survivors of
deceased veterans.
The Committee report estimated that of the total appropriation, $4,991,296,000 (75 percent) was for direct payments to veterans
and their survivors, $1,475,-376,000 (22 percent) was for medical services, and $184 million (3 percent) was for administrative
expenses. It pointed out that VA costs were “expected to continue to increase” because 4 million new veterans were added to
the rolls as a result of the 1966 legislation, and about 550,000 new veterans a year were being added as men were discharged
from the armed services.
Conservative New England Republican Had Key Role in Saving Model Cities Funds From Defeat in House
An investigation by Congressional Quarterly uncovered the major reasons why the House voted to provide funds to get President
Johnson's model cities program under way while denying the President money to expand his fledgling rent supplements program.
Discussions with Washington lobbyists and with Administration and Capitol Hill sources made it clear that the “conservative
coalition” in the House likely would have killed both programs had not one of the coalition's key members defected on the model
cities plan. This member – Rep. Louis C. Wyman (R N.H.), one of the most conservative of House Republicans – cast the deciding
vote that saved the plan in subcommittee and followed up by rallying crucial votes for it on the House floor.
Other important factors, according to CQ's sources, were vigorous lobbying efforts in behalf of the model cities plan by big-city
mayors and leading business executives and the choice the Administration made concerning which of the programs it wanted
most.
Faced with the old adage that the House can't digest more than one controversial issue in a single bill, the Administration
focused its lobby effort on model cities. Fence-straddling Members who contended they couldn't go on record in favor of both
controversial programs were told their vote was needed most on the cities plan. Although the Administration hoped such
Members simply would stay away when the roll was called on rent supplements, many of them covered their “aye” votes on cities
with a “no” on the supplements test.
With Viet Nam war demands increasing, the Administration feared it had to be “now or never” for the model cities plan. Action
funds for the program were authorized only for two fiscal years – fiscal 1968 and 1969. Further escalation of the war could mean
an even tougher appropriations fight in the next session of Congress, making it conceivable that the cities authorization could
expire before the first project ever got under way. on the other hand, the supplements program already was off to a modest start
and Congress was committed to it – at least to a limited extent. The Federal Housing Administration already had Congressional
approval to enter into 40-year contracts with nonprofit groups to pay up to $32 million a year in supplements.
Largely because of these developments, cities funds narrowly were sustained May 17 while supplements took a 62-vote setback.

ROLE OF REP. WYMAN
Despite other factors working in favor of model cities, the program might well have been dropped had it not been for the
surprising help of a normally conservative Republican, Rep. Louis C. Wyman (N.H.). Wyman was one of the 10 members of the
HUD – Independent Offices Appropriations Subcommittee which had original jurisdiction over the appropriations bill.
Administration and GOP sources said they would have thought Wyman one of the least likely of GOP Members to support the
plan. (Only 35 of 176 GOP Representatives who voted did.) In addition to supporting Barry M. Gold-water, the Republicans'
stanchly conservative 1964 Presidential candidate, Wyman had been the only New England Representative to oppose the 1964
Civil Rights Act and had described the 1965 medicare legislation as a “deceptive hoax.” He almost always supported the
“conservative coalition” before he lost his re-election bid in 1964. (1964 Almanac p. 752)
Analysis of a key vote on the cities plan in subcommittee showed the appropriation probably could not have survived its first test
without Wyman's support. After the panel had rejected, by a 4–8 vote, a motion to approve the entire $662 million allotment,
Wyman voted with the members who had been expected to support the program to produce a 6–6 tie on a motion to knock out
all but the $12 million in planning funds. Under House rules, a tie does not carry. (Voting in favor of dropping the funds were

Lobby groups and other sources close to the issue said Wyman's support again was crucial on the House floor. Describing the

might not be “precisely the correct program,” he said, HUD and Congress could improve it through experience gained once the

Michel's recommittal motion, a shift of 11 votes could have spelled the difference.
Following House action, Wyman told CQ he had supported the plan because it would be a “grievous political error” for
Republicans to oppose aid to the nation's cities without proposing a constructive alternative. While rent supplements (which he
vigorously opposed) had a generally unpopular connotation in the public mind, he said, the model cities plan was associated with

Mayors. With the exception of the Administration's pressure effort, it was thought the strongest lobbying on Impetus for the
mayors' support came from efforts by the “Urban Alliance” – an ad hoc group of organizations which were generally favorable to

Realtors. A surprise development which some observers said hurt the supplements plan was the failure of the National Assn. of
Real Estate Boards (NAREB) – a powerful proponent of supplements – to mobilize its local boards in behalf of new funds for the
program. A top NAREB official told CQ the organization did not like to go to its local boards “too often” and felt there were “more
compelling issues” such as open housing (which it opposed) coming up later in the session. Moreover, the spokesman said,
NAREB felt it had done enough by wiring key Members of the House in quest of their support.
EFFECT OF 1966 GOP ELECTION GAINS
Rent Supplements. Although the Administration's decision to focus its attention on model cities undoubtedly hurt the
supplements plan, it was difficult to conceive of a victory for the program in light of the 47–seat GOP gain in the 1966 elections
to the House. In the preceding Congress, despite a Democratic majority of 155 Members, the program barely survived with major
modifications:
Although GOP support for supplements funds rose from six votes on each of the 1966 funding tests to 12 on the 1967 vote, there
were 41 more GOP opposition votes on the 1967 show-down than the average of 122 GOP “nays” on the two roll-call votes in
1966. Democratic opposition remained about the same but Democratic support diminished by 30 votes from the average of 189
in favor of the program in 1966. (See vote 54, p. 26-H; 1966 votes 4 and 5, 1966 Almanac p. 248.)
Model Cities. While rent supplements had been a big issue in the 1966 campaign, model cities had not been quite as
controversial. As a consequence of this and the other factors discussed in this box, Administration forces were able to maintain a
margin for funds that was only slightly diminished from the margin they commanded in 1966.
