Features
Congress Dec. 12 cleared for the President's signature a bill (S 2864), the Housing and Urban Development Act of 1969,
extending programs for one- and two-year periods under the Housing and Urban Development Act of 1968 and authorizing $4.8
billion for housing and urban development programs. The bill increased funds and relaxed cost ceilings for housing programs for
low- and moderate-income families.
Although the bill largely extended existing programs covering mortgage loans and insurance, urban renewal and public housing
assistance, model cities and urban development, it also contained a new provision, which had been added on the House floor, to
require that for each slum home razed as part of an urban renewal project, a new low-income dwelling unit had to be constructed.
The $4.8-billion authorization for fiscal 1970 and 1971 in the bill was $100 million less than the House authorization of $4.9 billion
and $1.4 billion less than the $6.2-billion total the Senate had approved for fiscal years 1970, 1971 and 1972.
The conferees extended the authority for the programs in the Act for only one year, in most cases through fiscal 1971 for
programs scheduled to expire in fiscal 1970, rather than for two years as the Senate had provided. House Members said that the
Administration was preparing a comprehensive housing bill for 1970 consideration that could modify or change existing programs.
The bill contained a number of provisions aimed at increasing housing for low-income persons. It increased the fiscal 1971 and
1972 contract authority for the home-ownership and rental assistance programs; raised room-cost limitations for public housing
projects; reduced rentals for very low-income tenants of public housing projects; and increased direct loan authority for housing of
the elderly.
A Senate proposal to establish flexible mortgage rate ceilings on Federal Housing Administration (FHA) programs and public
housing was dropped by the conferees in favor of a House provision establishing percentage increases over the ceilings in
existing law.
PROVISIONS—As cleared, S 2864 (PL 91 - 152):
Extended until  Oct. 1, 1971, the authority of the FHA to insure mortgage loans in homeownership programs for low-income families
and in construction or rehabilitation of multifamily housing projects for low-income families, and extended other mortgage credit
authorizations until  Oct. 1, 1970.
Lowered the down payment required on FHA-insured homes valued at $25,000 from 20 percent to 10 percent.
Authorized FHA financing of mobile homes for up to $10,000 with repayment over 12 years.
Increased by 10 percent the dollar limitations on FHA-insured sales and rental housing; by 15 percent on FHA-insured multifamily
dwelling units for low- and moderate-income families; and by 20 percent for FHA-insured low-income homeownership and rental
assistance housing and low-to-moderate income housing.
Increased from $100 million to $125 million the amounts authorized in fiscal 1970 and 1971 in contract authority for the Section 235
low-income homeownership program and the Section 236 low-income rental assistance program. Authorized $170 million in contract
authority for each program for fiscal 1972.
Authorized the Secretary of HUD to increase from 20 percent to 40 percent the maximum number of rent supplement units in any
Section 236 single-family dwelling project.

Background
REFERENCES—1965 Almanac p. 358; 1966 Almanac p. 210, 245; 1968 Almanac p. 313.
Congress in 1968 authorized a $5.3-billion, three-year housing program (PL 90-448) designed to provide more than 1.7 million
units of new and rehabilitated housing for low-income families.
Major new programs authorized by the measure included federal subsidies to help the poor buy their own homes and to rent
apartments, federal underwriting of the insurance industry against riot losses, and new urban renewal programs. The bill also
extended the rent supplement and model cities programs enacted by the 89th Congress. (Far summary of 1969 urban affairs
development, see p. 387, for related hearings, see p. 399.)

Amendments of 1969.

Administration bill proposed no new programs but rather an extension of several due to expire Oct. 1, 1969, and the consolidation
of other programs.

One major change in authority proposed by the Administration, Romney said, was a request for open-end authorizations for the
following programs: urban renewal, model cities, supplementary rental grants, planning grants, open space land grants and low-
income home-ownership and rental housing. (Under such authorizations, no specific funds or ceiling on funds are stipulated; the
Administration is free to request “such sums as are necessary” in appropriation bills.)
The Administration proposed a one-year extension through fiscal 1971 of the water and sewer program, neighborhood facilities
and advance acquisition of land for public purposes, Romney said.
Another program change called for in the bill, Romney said, was a provision permitting existing room-cost limits in the low-rent
public housing program to. be increased by up to 45 percent in high-cost areas.
July 17—Eugene A. Gulledge, president of the National Association of Home Builders, recommended that permanent authority be
given to the Secretary of HUD to insure mortgages through the Federal Housing Administration (FHA). The Administration
requested a two-year extension.
Gulledge also proposed permanent authority for the Secretary to set the FHA interest rate at a level necessary to meet market
conditions.
July 18—Jesse Gray, chairman of the National Tenants Organization, said public housing tenants were “no longer passive
consumers of housing.”
The rent scales for public housing, Gray said, should be at a level the poor could afford, or 15 percent of income for those below
the poverty level. Gray said the Department of HUD preferred for public housing authorities to raise rents to break even financially
rather than provide money to help the authorities lower rents.
William L. Rafsky, president of the National Association of Housing and Redevelopment Officials, said the organization was
opposed to Administration proposals to make new authorizations general, with no specific dollar allocation, or to limit extension of
new authorizations for a one-year period only.
If the one-year authorizations in S 2620 were enacted, Rafsky said, the Association proposed that legislation in 1970 extend
authorizations for all housing and urban development programs for at least three years.
John Evans, director of the Department of Urban Affairs of the AFL-CIO, said that only one out of every five new houses was built
by union labor and, therefore, large wage settlements by building trade unions could not be blamed for the high cost of housing.
Instead he said that high interest rates, which he called a “national disaster,” were responsible. Evans recommended a program
of direct federal loans to ease the shortage of low- and moderate-income housing.
Richard B. Green, director-of Cleveland's Department of Community Development, accused Secretary of Housing and Urban
Development George Romney of dragging his feet in spending money already appropriated by Congress for housing. Green said
that the Administration's interest in balancing the budget as part of its effort to curb inflation had taken precedence over the
urgent problems of the cities.
July 21—Mayor Thomas N. Urban (D) of Des Moines, Iowa, said Congress should consider requiring banks to channel some of
their funds into Government-aided housing to help meet the shortage of funds for housing. The Government could control the
interest rate at which the money would be lent, Urban said, to permit the banks to earn “an adequate—but not a maximum—rate
of profit.” Urban testified on behalf of the National League of Cities and the U.S. Conference of Mayors.
July 22—Philip N. Brownstein, of the Council of Housing Producers, recommended that the Secretary of the Department of Housing
and Urban Development (HUD) be given authority to raise mortgage limits by up to 45 percent in high-cost areas under the
homeowner-ship program for low-income families. (Under the program, the Government pays an interest rate subsidy to the
institution making the housing loan.) The high costs of land, labor, materials and financing in metropolitan areas made it
impossible, Brownstein said, for builders to produce houses within the existing $17,500 mortgage limits set by the Government for
high-cost areas.
Brownstein, Assistant Secretary for mortgage credit at HUD from 1966 to 1968, also proposed that the HUD Secretary be
empowered to raise income limits for the homeownership program when higher housing costs required higher mortgage
payments. Existing law provided that persons applying for the program have incomes no higher than 135 percent of the
maximum allowed for public housing.
Lon Worth Crow Jr., president of the Mortgage Bankers of America, criticized the Administration for failing to appoint an Assistant
Secretary for mortgage credit and a Federal Housing Administration (FHA) commissioner.
The Mortgage Bankers also disagreed with the Administration on its proposal for a two-year extension of FHA authority to insure
loans, Crow said, preferring a permanent extension instead. The association also recommended the removal of maximum dollar
limits on mortgages insured by FHA. The limits had been modified periodically during the years as rising prices increased the
cost of housing, Crow pointed out.
July 25—New York Mayor John V. Lindsay (R) warned the Subcommittee that “crisis conditions” existed in housing and called the
Administration bill inadequate to deal with the problem.
Publicly assisted housing production faced “imminent collapse,” Lindsay said, because of statutory cost limitations controlling both
low- and moderate-income housing construction. Construction costs more than doubled in the last 20 years, the New York Mayor
said, but cost limits for the public housing program were raised by only 26 percent in high-cost areas. The Administration bill,
Lindsay charged, entirely ignored FHA cost limits and increased public housing limits by only a fraction of what was needed. Fixed
dollar limitations on housing construction, Lindsay added, were “unrealistic and unworkable.”

ACTION—The full Committee Sept. 5 reported a bill (S 2864—S Rept 91-392), the Housing and Urban Development Act
Amendments of 1969. The bill extended most programs under the 1968 Act, which were scheduled to expire in fiscal 1970, for
two years, through fiscal 1972.
The Committee recommended $5,489,500,000 in new authorizations for fiscal 1971 and 1972 and also approved $702.5 million in
new contract authority for housing programs.
The bill was a substitute for the Administration proposal (S 2620) on which the Committee held hearings in July.
The Committee made three major changes in the public housing program:
Provided that families with no income could move into public housing with the Government paying their rent.
Provided that the Government would make up the difference if rent for a low-income family in public housing was more than 25 percent
of that family's income.
Raised the ceiling on the amounts that could be spent for per-room construction costs.
The existing rent-subsidy program applied to low-income persons living in private housing. The Government paid the portion of
the rent that was above 25 percent of the family's income.
Major Provisions—As reported, S 2864 included the following provisions:
Mortgage Credit:
Extended for two years (until  Oct. 1, 1971) the authority of the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) to insure mortgage loans.
Authorized the Secretary of the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) to increase mortgage limits on FHA-insured
housing by up to 45 percent in high-cost areas.
Increased contract authority for the homeowner-ship (Section 235) and rental assistance (Section 236) programs for low-income
families by $170 million each for fiscal 1972. Existing contract authority provided $125 million for each program for fiscal 1971.
Increased by $82 million in fiscal 1972 the contract authority for the rent supplement program.
Urban Renewal and Housing Assistance:
Increased the authorization for urban renewal by $1.3 billion in fiscal 1971 and by $1.7 billion in fiscal 1972. The $1.3 billion
authorization, the Committee said, was in addition to a carry-over of $587.5 million.
Added $20 million to the $150 million already authorized in annual contributions contracts for the low-rent public housing program in
fiscal 1971. The Committee earmarked the additional $20 million for modernization of existing public housing projects.
Authorized the Secretary to increase the basic per-room construction cost limits for public housing projects by up to 45 percent in high-
cost areas.
Authorized rental assistance payments to families in public housing to enable them to pay no more than 25 percent of their income for
rent. The provision also provided that families with no income could move into public housing with the Government paying their rent.
The Committee recommended a $75-miilion authorization for the program.
Authorized $80 million for each of fiscal years 1970-1972 for direct loans for housing of the elderly.
Model cities and metropolitan development programs:
Authorized $287.5 million for fiscal 1971 and $1.5 billion for fiscal 1972 for the model cities program. The $287.5 million added to a
carry-over of $712.5 million would provide a $l-billion program level in 1971, the Committee said.
Authorized the following additional increases: $40 million for comprehensive planning grants, $88 million for open space and urban
beautification and $34 million for the neighborhood facilities program for fiscal 1972.

HELD HEARINGS—Between July 21 and July 30 on an Administration bill (HR 12937), the Housing and Urban Development Act
Amendments of 1969.
TESTIMONY—July 22—John H. Tolan Jr., deputy for development of the city of San Francisco, testified on behalf of Mayor Joseph
L. Alioto (D). The Mayor recommended the following changes in housing legislation:
Permit non-elderly individuals who are needy to qualify for federally assisted housing. Such housing was presently available only
for the needy elderly and low-income families.
Authorize the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) to refinance multifamily housing as well as single-family homes. FHA

July 24—Nathaniel S. Keith, president of the National Housing Conference, said declining housing production and delayed urban
renewal action could partly be blamed on Administration and Congressional failure to fund urban programs adequately and to

Floor Action
The House Oct. 23 by a 339-9 roll-call vote passed HR 13827. (See vote 115, p. 58-H.)
The House-passed bill authorized some $4.9 billion in housing programs.
Following passage of the bill, the House by voice vote substituted the language of HR 13827 for the language of S 2864 as
passed by the Senate Sept. 23. The House then passed S 2864, as amended, by voice vote.
The House passed S 2864 after three days of debate during which $1.8 billion was added to the $3.1 billion recommended by
the House Banking and Currency Committee. Of the increase, $1.5 billion was authorized for the Government National Mortgage
Association (GNMA) to purchase mortgages on low-cost housing insured by the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) and the
Veterans Administration (VA).
The largest single item in the House-passed bill was $2 billion for the fiscal 1970 urban renewal program. Another $300 million
was added by the House for urban mass transit programs.
Before final passage of the bill, the House by a 116-92 teller vote recommitted the bill to the Banking and Currency Committee
with instructions to report it back to the floor with an amendment requiring urban renewal projects to provide for an equal amount
of housing to replace any housing demolished by the project. The bill was immediately reported back to the floor with the
amendment, and the amendment then was adopted by voice vote.
Offered by freshman Member Lowell P. Weicker Jr. (R Conn.), the amendment had been defeated Oct. 22 by a 26-27 standing
vote and 48-50 teller vote. Weicker said the urban renewal program had no requirement that housing be part of any project, only
that if housing were included in a community's plan it had to include 20 percent for low-or moderate-income families. The urban
renewal program “really could be called the great shopping plaza renewal act or the office building renewal act,” Weicker said.
Thomas L. Ashley (D Ohio) charged that the provision would wreck the urban renewal program by superimposing a judgment from
Washington over that of a local community. Some communities try to reduce density, Ashley said, by not replacing the
substandard housing units they remove. The effect of the amendment would be to lock into the cities those now living there, he

Conference Report
House and Senate conferees Dec. 10 filed a report (S Rept 91-740) on S 2864, authorizing a $4.8-billion authorization for
housing and urban development programs in fiscal 1971.
The conferees accepted the one-year extension through fiscal 1971 which had been approved by the House for most of the
programs, but added the Senate provision extending the interest subsidy programs for homeownership and rental housing
through fiscal 1972.
Retained in the conference report was a House provision requiring one-for-one replacement of housing demolished by urban
renewal. The provision had been added to the bill on the House floor after its sponsor, Lowell P. Weicker Jr. (R Conn.), charged
that the urban renewal program was being used to tear down low-income housing in order to build shopping plazas and office
buildings.
Conferees authorized $1.7 billion for urban renewal for fiscal 1971, compared to the House figure of $2 billion for fiscal 1971 and
the Senate provision for $1.3 billion for fiscal 1971 and $1.7 billion for fiscal 1972. (With a carryover of $587.5 million for fiscal
1971, the authorization for the program that year would total $2,287,500,000.)
For the model cities program, the compromise in the final bill provided an additional $600 million for the program in fiscal 1971, to
be added to a carryover of $712.5 million for a total of $1,312,500,000. The House had approved $750 million; the Senate,
$287.5 million for fiscal 1971 and $1.5 billion for fiscal 1972.
Both the House and Senate had sought to raise ceilings on FHA mortgage insurance programs after local officials had argued
that existing ceilings were unrealistic in the face of rising housing costs. The House had increased all FHA-insured per-dwelling-
unit and per-room dollar ceilings by 10 percent; the Senate based increases in mortgage limits for single- and multifamily housing
on the percentage change in average sales price of new homes since 1965. The Senate had also provided for a 45-percent
increase in per-room cost ceilings for low-income housing in high-cost areas. The conferees compromised on percentage
increases ranging from 10 percent to 20 percent for different housing programs.
The conferees dropped a House provision which reduced from 25 percent to 20 percent the amount of a tenant's income for
maximum rent under the Section 236 and rent supplement programs.
Retained in the final bill was the House version of an amendment added by both the House and Senate which prohibited
“unreasonable restraints” against the use of new technologies in housing. The Senate amendment had applied to all housing
under federal programs, but conferees accepted the House version which applied the restriction only to experimental housing
programs under the Operation Breakthrough program. Opponents of the provision had called it a “union-busting attempt” by
prohibiting union contracts against such new construction techniques as prefabrication.
Final Action
The House and the Senate Dec. 12 with little debate approved the conference report by voice votes.

90-Day Extension
Pending completion of the Housing and Urban Development Act Amendments of 1969 (S 2864), Congress passed a temporary
90-day extension of various housing laws which were scheduled to expire Oct. 1, 1969. The extension permitted Congress to
complete action on S 2864.
