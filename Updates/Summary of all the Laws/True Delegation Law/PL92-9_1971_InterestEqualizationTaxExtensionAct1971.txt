Interest Equalization
An article from CQ Almanac 1971
Congress March 30 cleared for the President's signature a bill (HR 5432—PL 92 - 9) providing a two-year extension, through
March 31, 1973, of the interest equalization tax.
Final action came when the House by voice vote approved the Senate version of the bill. The Senate had passed HR 5432 by a
voice vote earlier the same day but added two substantive amendments. The bill was passed by the House March 10 by a 393–5
roll-call vote.
The equalization tax applied to purchases of foreign securities by Americans and to loans by American banks to foreign
customers. Its purpose is to reduce the flow of capital abroad by discouraging foreigners from acquiring capital in the United
States and by reducing the desirability of foreign securities to Americans.
Background. The equalization tax was first enacted in 1964. It did not apply to U.S. direct investment in foreign businesses.
The tax previously had been extended twice, in 1967 and 1969. The 1969 Act (PL 91-128) expired March 31. The tax was
intended as a temporary weapon against the adverse U.S. balance-of-payments position following an increase in the private
long-term capital outflow from $2.88-billion in 1962 to $3.67-billion in 1963, an increase of 27 percent. The law was enacted by
Congress in 1964 and made retroactive to July 17, 1963, the date it was proposed by President Kennedy.
Originally, the law imposed a flat 15 percent tax rate, (equivalent to 1 percent annual interest) on U.S. purchases of foreign
stocks and bonds which had a maturity of 28 1/2 years or longer. A lower graduated rate was set for bonds with an earlier
maturity.
The 1964 Act gave the President discretionary power to apply the tax to bank loans with a maturity of one to three years. Later
that year, Congress extended the effective period of the Act to July 31, 1967, and also broadened the Act to include taxation of
other debt obligations which matured in one to three years.
In extending the Act in 1967 to July 31, 1969, the tax. rate was increased to 1.5 percent annual effective interest equivalent from
1 percent. The President also was given discretionary power to vary the tax rate from zero to 150 percent of the original 15
percent rate if he determined that adjustment was necessary to limit foreign security acquisitions to a level consistent with
balance-of-payments objectives.
Congress again renewed the tax in 1969 and amended the law by giving the President authority to reduce the tax on new foreign
security issues without reducing the rate on outstanding securities.
References. Congress and the Nation Vol. I p. 384; 1967 Almanac p. 255; 1969 Almanac p. 334.
Provisions. As cleared by Congress, HR 5432:
Extended the interest equalization tax through March 31, 1973.
Authorized the President to apply the tax, at his discretion, to bank loans and other debt obligations with a maturity of less than one
year.
Eliminated an existing exemption from the tax for new investments made after March 24, 1971, by certain mutual funds.
Allowed companies to elect to treat their debt issues as subject to the tax, thus exempting interest on this obligation from the 30-
percent withholding tax.
Provided for tax-free direct investment by any U.S. company in a business engaged in lending or financing abroad where adequate
assurances were given that the amounts invested would not be used to acquire foreign securities.

Committee. The House Ways and Means Committee March 3 unanimously reported HR 5432 (H Rept 92–35). The bill was
supported by the Administration.
The committee approved the bill as introduced except for the addition of technical amendments to the existing law.
In its report, the committee said the continued deficit in the U.S. balance of payments necessitated further extension of the
interest equalization tax and that without the extension the deficit could increase significantly.
Floor. The House March 10, by a 393–5 roll-call vote, passed HR 5432 as reported by the committee. (Vote 11, p. 4-H)
Voting against were: James C. Cleveland (R N.H.), Philip M. Crane (R Ill.), Bob Eckhardt (D Texas), James A. McClure (R Idaho) and
Ogden R. Reid (R N.Y.).
Although only five votes were cast against HR 5432, several members expressed the view that the equalization tax should be
viewed only as a temporary measure.
Rep. Wilbur D. Mills (D Ark.), chairman of the Ways and Means Committee, said during debate: “The interest equalization tax is
still intended as a transitional measure during the period we are dealing with the more fundamental causes of our balance of
payments deficit.”
The ranking Republican on the committee, John W. Byrnes (R Wis.), added: “I do not think there is any question but that if this tax
were removed, it would increase the demands against our money market. Foreigners would come here to borrow out of the
limited amount of money that is available because our interest rates, high as they are, are still lower than European rates. Such
developments…could not help but exert upward pressure on our interest rates….”
Major Provision. As passed by the House, HR 5432:
Extended the interest equalization tax for two years, through March 31, 1973.
Senate
Committee. The Senate Finance Committee March 27 reports (S Rept 92–47) HR 5432 with amendments. The committee
reported HR 5432 by voice vote, according to a staff aide.
The committee, in addition to approving the extension passed by the House, gave the President discretionary authority to apply
the interest equalization tax to bank loans and other debt obligations with a maturity of less than one year. Existing law applied
only to maturities of one year or longer.
The committee also removed the equalization tax exempt status from new investments made after March 24, 1971, by certain
mutual funds.
Floor. The Senate March 30 passed HR 5432 by voice vote. The Senate accepted the committee amendments without
opposition. However, Jacob K. Javits (R N.Y.) said the standby authority had been opposed by the Treasury Department.
An amendment introduced by Birch Bayh (D Ind.) to extend federally financed emergency unemployment compensation an
additional 13 weeks was tabled by a 45–40 roll-call vote. The nongermane amendment was identical to a bill (S 973) which Bayh
introduced Feb. 25. (Vote 25, p. 6-S) (Story on unemployment compensation)
Under the expansion of the federal-state unemployment compensation program passed in 1970 (PL 91-373), 13 weeks of
additional compensation—beyond the normal 26 weeks—was provided, to be financed jointly by federal and state governments.
But in order for states to be eligible for the 50-percent financing by the federal government, state legislatures had to raise their
payroll taxes to provide matching funds. (1970 Almanac p. 1108)
Bayh's amendment would have provided total federal financing of the additional compensation when a rate of 4-percent
unemployment was reached among workers covered by the program. Under the 1970 Act, the 13 weeks of emergency benefits
were to be triggered at a 4.5-percent unemployment rate.
The amendment would have put the emergency compensation into effect immediately rather than in 1972, as provided in the Act
and in some state laws.
Before voting on the amendment, Bayh accepted a modification proposed by Javits. Javits sought to make the effect of the bill
retroactive for states that had already enacted legislation providing for the 13 weeks emergency compensation. The modification
would have applied to about 14 states including New York.
Russell B. Long (D La.), who offered the motion to table the Bayh amendment, said the nongermane amendment should not be
added to the interest equalization extension. “If the Senate wants to add something of this sort, it should be done when we have
the Social Security bill before us…. If this amendment is to he acted upon, we should at least have an opportunity in committee
to know that it is going to be added so that all those affected can have an opportunity to be heard,” Long said.
