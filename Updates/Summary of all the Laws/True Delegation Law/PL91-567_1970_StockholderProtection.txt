Protection for Stockholders

Congress Dec. 10 cleared for the President's signature a bill (S 3431—PL 91
- 567 ) to protect stockholders during corporate takeover attempts,
chiefly by broadening the reporting requirements of existing law governing
efforts to acquire significant proportions of a company's stock.
Final action came when the House by voice vote concurred in a Senate
amendment to the House version of the bill. The House, in passing the bill
Dec. 7, approved an amendment added to the bill by its Interstate and
Foreign Commerce Committee (below). The Senate by voice vote Dec. 9
concurred in the House amendment but added an amendment of its own.
References. Congress and the Nation Vol. II p. 277; 1968 Almanac p. 337.
Provisions. As cleared by Congress, S 3431:
Extended to acquisitions of more than 5 percent of the stock of a publicly held
company the reporting requirements in existing law applying to acquisitions of
more than 10 percent of such stock.
Extended similar reporting requirements to offers tendered to stockholders to
purchase stock which would result in holdings of more than 5 percent of a
company's stock (10 percent in existing law).
Extended the reporting requirements to acquisitions of insurance company
securities.
Extended to stock-for-stock exchange offers the requirements of existing law
applying to cash tender offers for stock purchases.
Broadened SEC rule-making authority to prevent fraudulent, deceptive and
manipulative practices.
Authorized the SEC to exempt stockholders owning a controlling interest in a
company from notification and reporting requirements applying to a company's
purchase of its own stock (existing law did not permit a distinction).
Authorized the SEC to establish simpler reporting requirements for persons
and firms acquiring more than 5 percent of a company's stock in the normal
course of business.
Restored the registration exemptions in previously existing law, which were
inadvertently omitted from a bill (S 2224) broadening regulation of mutual
funds, for state and municipal industrial development revenue bonds and
variable annuities offered by insurance companies.
Background. The Securities and Exchange Act of 1934, as amended in 1968
(PL 90-439), placed a reporting requirement on any person or company
acquiring more than 10 percent of the stock of a publicly held business with
$1 million or more of assets or of certain types of investment companies. (The
provision was not applicable to mutual funds.) The Act required that a report
be filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) disclosing
certain information.

The Act also placed a reporting requirement on any person or company
making an offer to purchase such securities which, if successful, would result
in holdings of more than 10 percent of the stock of a business. It required a
similar report to the SEC, the target company and the stockholders whose
shares were solicited.
In each case, the report was required to include the identity and background
of the person acquiring the securities, the source and amount of funds used,
the purpose of the acquisition and the amount of stock held.
Senate Action
The Senate Banking and Currency Committee Aug. 14 reported S 3431 (S
Rept 91 -1125) broadening the reporting requirements of the Act by
reducing the proportion of stock held or sought to 5 percent. (Hearings, box p.
914)
The Committee found that acquisitions of 5 to 10 percent of a company's
stock could have a significant impact on the public market for that stock. It
cited the SEC as the source of information that some persons and companies
intent on takeovers limited their open-market purchases to 8 or 9 percent to
avoid the reporting requirement.
The bill extended the reporting requirement to purchases of stock of insurance companies and broadened the SEC's
regulatory authority to prevent fraudulent, deceptive and manipulative practices. Noting the increased use of stock-
for-stock exchange offers, resulting from the often prohibitive capital requirements of cash offers for securities, the
Committee included a provision extending to exchange offers the provisions of existing law applying to cash tenders.
The bill did not attempt to prevent corporate takeovers. Its purpose was to enable stockholders to protect their own
interests by ensuring that they were informed of possible takeover attempts.
The Senate passed the bill by voice vote Aug. 18.
House Action
The House Interstate and Foreign Commerce. Committee Dec. 2 reported S 3431 (H Rept 91-1655). The
Committee's Commerce and Finance Subcommittee held a hearing on the bill Oct. 12 and received testimony from
SEC Chairman Hamer H. Budge.
The Committee added an amendment to the Senate-passed version of the bill which granted to the SEC authority to
permit simpler reporting requirements for persons and firms acquiring more than 5 percent of the stock of a
company in the ordinary course of business and not for the purpose of changing or influencing control of the
company. According to the Committee report, acquisitions by stock exchange specialists, over-the-counter stock
market-makers and investment companies, including mutual funds, might have been subject to the full reporting
requirements of the bill as passed by the Senate.
The House Dec. 7 passed S 3431 by voice vote under suspension of the rules, which requires a two-thirds vote and
prohibits floor amendments.
Final Action
Senate. The Senate Dec. 9 by voice vote accepted the House amendment to the bill. The Senate then added a
nongermane amendment by voice vote correcting two omissions in a bill (S 2224) broadening the regulation of
mutual funds. (Congress cleared S 2224 Dec. 1.) (p. 890)
The final provisions of S 2224 as agreed upon by a House-Senate conference inadvertently deleted provisions in
previously existing law exempting state and municipal industrial development revenue bonds and variable annuities
offered by insurance companies from the registration requirements of Federal securities laws. The Senate
amendment to S 3431 restored both.
House. The House Dec. 10 agreed to the Senate amendment to the bill by voice vote, clearing the legislation for
the President's signature.

The Senate Banking and Currency Subcommittee on Securities held a hearing March 25 on S 336 and S 3431,
both of which modified securities registration and disclosure requirements. (Stories, this page, p. 915)
Testimony March 25
Hamer H. Budge, chairman Securities and Exchange Commission, supported both bills:
Insurance companies were exempted from take-over disclosure requirements in 1968 because they were regulated
by state commissioners. However, tender offers for insurance companies are frequently made on a nationwide basis
and are not presently regulated by state commissioners.
Companies undertaking an acquisition have often limited the amount of stock they purchased to 9 percent to avoid
the requirement to file disclosures in purchases of 10 percent of a company's securities. Companies have also been
able to avoid the disclosure requirement by offering an exchange of securities rather than a cash tender.
Existing law prohibits fraudulent and deceptive practices and false statements in connection with tender offers but it
does not give the SEC authority to make rules for dealing with such practices. Rule-making authority would make
the SEC more effective in dealing with these practices.
Extending the securities registration exemption to securities valued at $500,000 or less would aid small business in
raising capital. The economic situation which prompted Congress to increase the limitation from $100,000 to
$300,000 in 1945 is identical to the economic situation today.
Richard B. Walbert, president of the National Association of Securities Dealers (NASD), supported both bills:
Brokers and dealers, however, should be exempted from the S 3431 requirement for filing disclosures when
purchasing 5 percent or more of a company's stock. The NASD also questions elimination of the exemption from
disclosure requirements for tender offers when the acquisitions are made by means of a registration statement.
Enactment of S 336 should help to reduce the workload of the SEC while providing adequate protection for
investors.
Craig Severance, representing the Investment Bankers Association, opposed S 336 but supported S 3431:
The $300,000 limit on securities issues which can be offered without a registration being filed should not be
increased. A high percentage of the small issues are extremely speculative, partly because they are issued by small
companies where the mortality rate is high. In this speculative market, the public needs the protection of registration
requirements.
Jordan H. Eskin, a New York financial consultant and attorney, opposed S 3431 because, he said, it would greatly
hinder investor proxy fights against inefficient management:
In lieu of favorably reporting S 3431, the Subcommittee should recommend studies to propose legislation to restore
investor property and voting rights, and to impose penalties on management which protects itself at the expense of
the company.
The Committee should also propose legislation to eliminate disclosure requirements on persons involved in takeover
bids.
