Truth in Leasing
An article from CQ Almanac 1976
The House and Senate March 9 cleared for the President a bill (HR 8835 ) to protect the growing number of
consumers who lease cars, furniture, appliances and other goods instead of paying cash or buying on credit. It was
intended to extend to lease contracts the disclosure and protection requirements imposed on credit transactions by
the 1968 Truth in Lending Act (PL 90-321).
The measure would require companies to disclose terms and payments in advance, so that consumers could
compare lease contracts with other buying plans and to protect them against unexpected “balloon” charges
demanded after monthly payments have been completed. President Ford signed the bill (PL 94-240) March 23.
Legislative History
The House passed its version of HR 8835  Oct. 28 by a vote of 339–41. (1975 Almanac p. 583)
The Senate Banking, Housing and Urban Affairs Committee Jan. 21 reported (S Rept 94–590) a somewhat different
version which the Senate passed by voice vote Feb. 2.
Joe Biden (D Del.), chairman of the committee's Consumer Affairs Subcommittee, said the bill had “consensus
support” from consumer groups and the leasing industry.
Leasing was growing fastest in the automobile area, Biden said. “Because leasing is an alternative to traditional
installment sales, consumers need adequate disclosure of the critical cost elements in leases, so they can compare
one lease with another and so they can compare leases with other financing options,” he said.
The only objection raised before the bill was approved came from Jesse A. Helms (R N.C.), who said it would hurt
consumers by increasing business costs and would “continue a broad pattern of federal encroachment into areas
that are properly state prerogatives.”
The conference report on HR 8835  (H Rept 94–872), filed March 4, reflected numerous changes in the details
of both the House and Senate versions.
The House bill set a limit on the amount a consumer could be required to pay after a lease expired. The conferees
adopted instead the Senate provision requiring companies to estimate the resale value of the leased item accurately
before the lease was signed, and putting the burden of proof on the company if the final charge exceeded the limit
included in the House bill—the cost of three monthly lease payments. The conferees wrote in additional legal
protections against excessive “balloon payments.”
The conferees adopted a House provision permitting citizens to sue companies that violated the lease advertising
requirements in the bill, modifying it to clarify that only someone actually damaged by the violation could sue. This
provision was not intended to be used “on a wholesale basis.” the conferees noted in their report. They said they
hoped the threat of lawsuits would be enough to discourage most unfair lease advertising.
The conferees adopted the Senate bill's higher ceiling on class action recoveries under the bill—$500,000 or 1 per
cent of the lessor's net worth, whichever was less. The House had recommended a ceiling of $100,000 or 1 per
cent of net worth, whichever was less. The increase in class action recoveries would apply to all suits brought under
the Truth in Lending Act.
The House and Senate adopted the conference report on the bill by voice votes March 9.
Provisions

A congressional finding that “there has been a recent trend toward leasing automobiles and other durable goods for

cost disclosures.”
A requirement that companies provide consumers in advance with written statements of lease terms—including amount
of monthly payments and other charges; information on warranties, guarantees and insurance; conditions for
termination of lease, and notification if the consumer will be liable for the difference between the item's actual and fair
market value at the expiration of the lease. If the consumer's liability at the expiration of a lease is based on a prior
estimate of the item's market value, the leasing company would be open to challenge if it charged the consumer more
than the cost of three monthly payments.
A prohibition against misleading lease advertising. A person who suffers actual damage because of misleading lease
advertising could sue for damages, but advertisers who make unintentional errors would be exempt from liability.
An increase in the ceiling on class action recoveries under the Truth in Lending Act to $500,000 or 1 per cent of a
lessor's net worth, whichever was less. (The existing ceiling was the lesser of $100,000 or 1 per cent of net worth.)
An exemption from the disclosure law for leases for agricultural, business or commercial purposes, or leases to a
government or governmental organization.
