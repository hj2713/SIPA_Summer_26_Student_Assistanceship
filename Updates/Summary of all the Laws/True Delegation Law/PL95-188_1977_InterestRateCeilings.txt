Interest Rate Ceilings
An article from CQ Almanac 1977
Congress completed action Nov. 2 on legislation (HR 9710 —PL 95-188 extending for one year, until Dec. 15,
1978, the authority of federal regulatory agencies to set ceilings on interest rates paid on savings accounts.
Under this “Regulation Q” authority, thrift institutions maintained a .25 per cent competitive edge over commercial
banks on savings account interest rates. Savings and loan associations and other thrift institutions argued that if the
authority were allowed to expire, competition with banks for the savings dollar would drain capital needed to finance
home mortgages. Savings and loans supply about 75 per cent of the money for home loans.
Congress had periodically extended Regulation Q since it was established in 1966, most recently in a bill dealing
with credit union powers enacted early in 1977. (Credit union powers, p. 153)
HR 9710  also included provisions requiring Senate confirmation of persons designated by the President as
chairman and vice chairman of the Federal Reserve Board and making permanent existing arrangements for
quarterly discussions of monetary policy between the Fed chairman and the Senate and House banking
committees. The Federal Reserve provisions were the remnants of ambitious “reform” legislation ( HR  8094)
advanced by House Democrats earlier in the year. (Background, 1976 Almanac p. 88, 1975 Almanac p. 166)
Provisions
As signed into law, HR 9710 :
Extended for one year, until  Dec. 15, 1978, the authority of federal regulatory agencies to set differential ceilings on
interest rates financial institutions can pay on savings accounts.
Required quarterly testimony by the Federal Reserve Board on general monetary policy targets before the Senate
Banking, Housing and Urban Affairs Committee and the House Banking, Finance and Urban Affairs Committee.
Required Senate confirmation of presidential nominees for chairman and vice chairman of the Federal Reserve Board.
By postponing the effective date of the provision until  Jan. 1, 1979, the bill exempted the incumbent chairman, Arthur F.
Burns, whose term as chairman was to expire Jan. 31, 1978.
Prohibited discrimination in selection of regional Federal Reserve Bank directors and required consideration of broader
economic interests in the selection of bank directors representing the public.
Extended federal conflict-of-interest law to cover the Federal Reserve, barring Fed officers and employees from acting
on matters in which they had a significant financial interest.
Extended from two to five years the period during which a financial institution may hold shares of a bank acquired
through debt collection before the Federal Reserve Board can force divestiture.
Shortened to 10 days from 30 days the waiting period required of the Federal Reserve Board before it can arrange for a
holding company to take over a failing bank in an emergency situation.
Legislative History
Final action on HR 9710  came Nov. 2 when the House agreed to a Senate amendment striking a provision
making the terms of the Federal Reserve chairman and vice chairman roughly coterminous with the President's
term. The Senate amendment was sponsored by John G. Tower (R Texas), who said coterminous terms would
jeopardize Fed independence.
