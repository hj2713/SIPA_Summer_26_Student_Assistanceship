Insider-Trading Bill
An article from CQ Almanac 1988

Congress cleared a bill (HR 5133 — PL 100 - 704 ) that significantly
increased both civil and criminal penalties for persons who traded stocks
using material, non-public information. The bill put new demands on firms to
police the activities of their employees, allowed private individuals to sue for
damages if they were financially injured by inside-traders' deals, and allowed
the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) to pay bounties for tips
leading to insider-trading investigations.
The House passed the measure unanimously Sept. 14, and the Senate
approved the House-passed bill by voice vote Oct. 22. The president signed
the bill Nov. 19.
The push for stiffer penalties came as more arrests and convictions resulted
from the Ivan F. Boesky case, which shook Wall Street in November 1986.
Just days before the House vote, the SEC had filed a lengthy complaint
against one of Wall Street's largest investment houses, Drexel Burnham
Lambert Inc.
Major Provisions
As cleared, HR 5133:
Civil Penalties. Allowed federal courts to impose penalties of up to three
times the profit gained, or the loss avoided, in cases brought by the SEC
alleging civil violations of insider-trading laws. The penalties could be imposed
on the person engaged in the illegal trading, on a person “tipping” another to
material, non-public information for the purpose of illegal insider trading, and
on a “controlling person,” typically a securities firm employing the trader or
tipper.
Penalties for controlling persons were limited to a maximum of $1 million or
three times the profit gained or loss avoided. And in the case of tippers, only
profits gained or losses avoided by the person directly intended to benefit
from the passing of information were covered.
For a controlling person to be liable for penalties, he would have to “know or
recklessly disregard” facts that a “controlled person” was violating the law.
Obligations of Securities Firms. Required securities dealers to maintain
policies “reasonably designed” to prevent illegal insider trading by the firm or
its employees. A knowing or reckless disregard of this requirement would
make securities firms liable for penalties if its employees engaged in illegal
insider trades.
Criminal Penalties. Increased the maximum jail term that could be imposed
in a criminal insider-trading case (brought by the Justice Department) from five
years to 10. The bill also increased the maximum criminal fine for individuals
convicted of insider trading from $ 100 ,000 to $1 million. Corporations
were subject to an increase in the maximum penalty from $ 100 ,000 to
$2.5 million.
Bounty. Authorized the SEC to pay bounties to persons who volunteered
information that might lead to insider-trading convictions. Awards, similar to

whether the foreign government had a reciprocal agreement to cooperate with
the SEC and whether cooperation would “prejudice the public interest of the
United States.” The SEC had sought authority to engage in international
cooperation because of the degree to which securities trading had evolved
into a common, cross-border market.
House Action
The bill was approved by the Finance Subcommittee of the House Energy
and Commerce Committee Aug. 4. The full committee gave its approval (H
Rept 100 -910), also by voice vote, Aug. 9, after agreeing by voice vote
to adopt a block of four amendments, offered by Chairman John D. Dingell, D-
Mich.
Although Dingell characterized the amendments as technical, two of them made substantive changes sought by
industry lobbyists.
One of Dingell's amendments limited the bill's specific “right of action” to cases where a private citizen and an inside
trader were buying or selling the same stock at the same time.
The earlier version of the bill would have created a broader right of action — for example, in cases where a person
traded on advance knowledge of a takeover attempt. Such trading could harm either the person attempting the
takeover or third-party stockholders.
But bill sponsor Edward J. Markey, D-Mass., said the language was too vague and created too many potential
problems. Eleventh-hour efforts to redraft the section were unsuccessful. So the provision creating the broader right
of action was removed.
Markey aides said that dropping the broader provision from the bill should not be read as a limit on judges allowing
private suits. “We do not want to freeze evolving case law,” Markey said.
The other significant change made by the committee limited the bill's coverage of tippers, persons who shared inside
information with the expectation that it would be used, but who did not themselves benefit from inside trades.
The change, according to aides, would prevent creation of an endless chain of legal responsibility back to the
original source of a single piece of information, if that person had no expectation that the ultimate recipient would
use the information to trade illegally in the market.
But tippers who passed along information and expected that it would be used illegally would be subject to the same
penalties as the person who traded with the tip.
Except for debate over the right-of-action and tipping provisions, the bill was virtually unopposed.
Even representatives of securities firms who would be subject to penalties for the actions of their employees did not
oppose it, although they had previously expressed concern over the paying of bounties.
On Sept. 14 — in the wake of the largest complaint of illegal insider trading ever brought by the SEC — the House
voted unanimously for the bill. The 410-0 vote was seen in part as a reaction to the SEC's Sept. 7 charges against
the investment firm of Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. Those charges were an outgrowth of the earlier insider-trading
scandal that centered on Ivan F. Boesky. (Vote 314, p. 96-H)
Insider trading, said Markey, was “a deplorable practice that has undermined the public trust in the fundamental
fairness and integrity of our securities markets.”
“The timing couldn't have been better,” agreed a Markey aide.

affiliates to get into the securities business. (Bank powers, p. 230)

Senate Banking also had approved a bill (S 2544 — S Rept 100-461) July 27 that dealt only with improving
international cooperation in securities-fraud cases.
The House vote seemed to give HR 5133 a push forward, but there was concern that the Senate panel might try to
use the bill as a vehicle for other proposals, such as limiting hostile corporate takeovers.
Perhaps more significantly, members of the Senate committee were unhappy that Energy and Commerce Chairman
Dingell was not inclined to go along with S 1886. Dingell had said he was not unalterably opposed to the idea, but
he was expected to seek limits on securities activities that might eventually kill that bill.
Senate Banking Chairman William Proxmire, D-Wis., who had pressed hard for the bank-deregulation measure, said
Sept. 14 he would not hold the insider-trading bill hostage to the banking measure. But aides to the chairman left
the clear impression that it would be used as leverage in negotiations over the banking bill.
But in the final days of the session, it became clear that the bank deregulation bill was dead. That broke a months-
long legislative logjam, and the insider-trading bill was cleared by voice vote and with no debate.
