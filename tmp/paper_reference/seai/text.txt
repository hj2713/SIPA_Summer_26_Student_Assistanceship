WP2 develops the Policy Intelligence Agent. Its role is to convert Irish and EU climate, energy, AI,
infrastructure, and environmental policy documents into structured variables for modelling, scenario
testing, and policy analysis.
The policy problem is practical. Ireland has climate and energy targets, but their delivery depends on
institutions, timelines, legal authority, reporting duties, enforcement mechanisms, infrastructure
capacity, and the ability to adapt to uncertainty. The agent needs to identify not only formal targets
but also the governance conditions that affect whether those targets can be implemented.
Exhibit WP2.1 outlines the human-in-the-loop process for building and validating the policy
intelligence layer. The PhD student and research support will develop the policy and legal corpus, tag
documents with metadata, and apply a coding framework developed with guidance from the senior
policy team. The coding framework will identify policy objectives, responsible actors, legal authority,
implementation timelines, reporting obligations, review clauses, enforcement mechanisms, data
requirements, risk flags, and affected sectors.

A manually coded validation sample will be used to evaluate the Policy Intelligence Agent. The agent
will extract obligations, actors, timelines, targets, risks, and resilience mechanisms from policy and
legal text. The extracted outputs will be checked against the hand-coded sample. Errors, ambiguous
classifications, and missing variables will be logged and used to improve the codebook, prompts, and
extraction schema.
The policy team will review difficult classifications, especially when legal authority, institutional
responsibility, accountability, enforcement, or implementation risk is unclear. Incorporating domain
and policy experts into the evaluation process provides quality assurance and ensures that AI-
generated outputs are grounded in expert policy analysis.
The final outputs will be validated policy variables, including policy constraints, implementation
timelines, governance-risk flags, institutional responsibilities, accountability mechanisms, and
resilience indicators. These variables will support WP3 energy-system modelling, scenario stress-
testing, implementation-gap analysis, policy briefs, and stakeholder guidance.
The policy team’s contribution directly strengthens the proposal’s core objective: integrating policy
intelligence, emissions analytics, and energy-system optimisation into a decision-support framework
for Ireland. The aim is to assess whether a decarbonisation pathway is technically feasible and,
equally important, to evaluate its institutional credibility, resilience to shocks, and usefulness for
public-sector planning.
