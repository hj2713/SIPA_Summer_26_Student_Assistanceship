Reserve Requirements
An article from CQ Almanac 1959

The principal provision of S 1120, dubbed the “vault cash ” bill, authorized
the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System to permit member
banks to count cash  on hand toward meeting their reserve requirements. 
Cash  held by all member banks in November 1958 totaled $2.2 billion; of
that sum, $1.3 billion was held by 6,007 members designated as country
banks, amounting to almost 25 percent of their required reserves. By
permitting member banks, and particularly country banks, to count this cash
 in satisfying their reserve requirements, the Board of Governors intended
to eliminate certain inequities in the reserve structure and to provide greater
flexibility within the system.
S 1120 also amended the law under which reserve requirements of member
banks are decreed by the Board of Governors within statutory ranges for each
of three categories: country banks, reserve city banks, and central reserve
city banks. For country banks, the legal range was from 7 to 14 percent of
demand deposits; for 274 reserve city banks in 49 reserve cities, from 10 to
20 percent of demand deposits; and for 32 central reserve city banks in New
York and Chicago, 13 to 26 percent. S 1120 lowered the minimum and
maximum limits for central reserve city banks to 10 and 22 percent,
respectively, and raised the maximum limit for reserve city banks from 20 to 22 percent. It also provided for
termination of the “central reserve city” classification in three years, after which banks in that category would be
grouped with reserve city banks.
PROVISIONS – As enacted, S 1120:
Authorized the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System
to permit member banks to “count all or part of their currency and
coin as reserves.”
Reduced the minimum and maximum reserve limits for central
reserve city banks from 13 and 26 percent, respectively, to 10 and
22 percent of demand deposits, and raised the maximum limit for
reserve city banks to 22 percent of demand deposits.
Authorized the Board to permit a reserve city or central reserve city
member bank to carry lower reserves than other banks in the same
city, based upon the nature of the bank's business rather than, as
in the past, its geographical location.
Terminated, effective July 28, 1962, the “central reserve cities”
classification.
Authorized the Board to designate which holding company affiliate
(where there is more than one with respect to the same bank or
group of banks) is to establish and maintain the reserves required
by law.

ACTION – April 17 reported S 1120 (S Rept 195) with three provisions: those, as finally enacted, authorizing the
application of vault cash  to reserves and the lowering of reserve requirements for individual banks (the central
reserve maximum was reduced to 20 percent in this version), and one terminating immediately the central reserve
city classification. It was not the purpose of the bill, the report stated, “to interfere with the monetary policy
objectives of the Federal Reserve Board.”
Floor Action
The Senate passed S 1120 May 13, by voice vote, after agreeing, by voice vote, to an amendment by Sen. Prescott
Bush (R Conn.) postponing for three years the termination of the central reserve city classification. Earlier Bush had
modified his amendment, at the request of Sen. Jacob K. Javits (R N.Y.), to include the provision respecting holding
company reserves that was contained in the final version.
House
COMMITTEE – Banking and Currency.
ACTION – May 28 reported S 1120 (H Rept 403) in the form approved by the Senate, but minus the holding
company provision. The report noted that the Board of Governors had testified they intended to put the “ vault
cash ” provision “into effect gradually, so as to avoid the disturbing effect upon our economy that would result from
suddenly increasing reserves by the $2 billion now held in vault cash .” (The first step was taken Dec. 1.)
In a lengthy dissent, Rep. Wright Patman (D Texas) described S 1120 as the “bond giveaway” bill, contending it
would result in transferring “to the private banks about $15 billion worth of U.S. bonds and other interest-bearing
obligations of the United States which are now owned by the public.” Reps. Henry S. Reuss (D Wis.) and Byron L.
Johnson (D Colo.) opposed reduction of the maximum reserve requirement for central reserve city banks from 26 to
20 percent, saying “it is far better for the Federal Reserve to have the power and not need it, than to need the
power and not have it.”
Floor Action
The House July 1 passed S 1120 by voice vote, after rejecting, by a 60–309 roll-call vote, a motion to recommit the
bill to the Banking and Currency Committee for further study. (For voting, see chart p. 374) An amendment by Rep.
Reuss, raising the maximum reserve requirement for reserve city and central reserve city banks to 22 percent, was
agreed to by voice vote.
Amendment Accepted
Henry S. Reuss (D Wis.) – Raise the ceiling on reserve requirements for reserve city banks from 20 to 22 percent of
demand deposits; July 1. Voice vote.
Amendments Rejected
Byron L. Johnson (D Colo.) – Amend Reuss amendment by setting maximum at 26 percent; July 1. Standing, 29–74.
Johnson – Add statement that “the purpose of this act is solely to permit the Board of Governors to remove
inequities;” July 1. Standing, 37–71.
Wright Patman (D Texas) – Delete all of the bill except the vault cash provision; July 1. Standing 30–100.
Patman – Require a transfer of $15 billion in Government obligations from Federal Reserve banks to the Treasury;
July 1. Voice.
Conference
Conferees on S 1120 agreed (H Rept 651) to accept the Javits holding company provision, added by the Senate,
and the Reuss maximum reserve amendment, added by the House. The report was agreed to by the House July 14
and the Senate July 15, by voice votes.
RELATED DEVELOPMENT: Congress Aug. 26 completed action on another bill (HR 8160) liberalizing lending and
borrowing powers of national banks. As signed into law Sept. 9 (PL 86–251 ), the bill: increased the ceiling on a
bank's borrowing authority from 100 percent of capital to 100 percent of capital plus 50 percent of surplus; liberalized
exceptions to the rule that a bank can lend no more than 10 percent of its capital and surplus to one borrower;
liberalized some of the restrictions on real estate loans by national banks; and authorized appointment of an
additional Deputy Comptroller of the Currency. Most of these provisions had been part of the Financial Institutions
Act of 1957, passed by the Senate but never acted upon by the House. (1957 Almanac p. 675)
