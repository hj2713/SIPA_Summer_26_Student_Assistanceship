Interest, Credit Controls
An article from CQ Almanac 1969
Document Outline
Senate
Conference Action
Congress in 1969 passed legislation (S 2577—PL 91–151) to increase the
supply of credit  during tight money periods and to strengthen the
execution of monetary policies.
The bill in its major provisions authorized the President to instruct the Federal
Reserve Board to regulate terms, amounts and interest  rates on all forms
of credit  when he deemed it necessary to combat inflation; authorized an
alternative system of controls  under which the President could ask the 
credit  industry to work out restraints voluntarily; and raised maximum
insurance protection on deposits in federally chartered banks and savings and
loan institutions from $15,000 to $20,000. The measure also extended flexible
authority for federal banking regulatory agencies to set interest  rates for
fixed maturity deposits (such as “certificates of deposit”) in banks and savings
and loan institutions.
BACKGROUND—Congress first authorized regulatory agencies to set
maximum interest  rates on deposits in commercial banks and savings
and loan associations in 1966 to curb excessive competition for deposits
between financial institutions. Unrestrained bidding was reducing the savings
flow into thrift institutions, the main suppliers of mortgage credit . As a
result the institutions were forced to cut back mortgage lending, contributing to a rapid decline in new building
projects. The authority was extended in 1967 and 1968. (1966 Almanac p. 753; 1967 Almanac p. 84)
Controversy arose in 1968 over an amendment offered by Sen. William Proxmire (D Wis.) directing the Federal
Reserve Board (FRB) to purchase directly obligations of Government agencies when such actions would aid the
housing market. The amendment, adopted in Committee, was dropped after the Treasury Department and the FRB
strongly opposed the proposal.
Senate
COMMITTEE—Senate Banking and Currency; John J. Sparkman (D Ala.), chairman.
ACTION—Nov. 5 reported with amendments a bill (S 2577—S Rept 91–516) to provide additional mortgage credit
 during periods of tight money and to strengthen the execution of monetary policies. The bill was introduced July
9 by Proxmire, chairman of the Subcommittee on Financial Institutions. Hearings were held by the Subcommittee on
Sept. 9, 10 and 22. In executive session Sept. 18, the Subcommittee recommended the bill with amendments to the
full Committee, which on Oct. 22 ordered it reported with additional amendments.
In a substitute for his 1968 approach, Proxmire proposed and the Committee adopted language expressing the
“intent of Congress” that the Secretary of the Treasury use authority to advance funds to Home Loan Banks at rates
consistent with market rates, if other alternatives were ineffective, to permit members of the Federal Home Loan
Bank (FHLB) to continue to supply mortgage funds when rising interest  rates or tight money situations
prevented other institutions from doing so.
The National Association of Homebuilders and other housing associations strongly supported S 2577 and urged
extension of PL 89–597 permanently. They asked that the Treasury be authorized to lend the Home Loan Bank up
to $5 billion, instead of the $1 billion originally authorized in the bill.
Provisions. As reported, S 2577:
Extended until  Sept. 22, 1970, the authority to establish flexible ceilings on rates paid by financial institutions on time
and savings deposits.

Extended, effective July 31, 1970, federal deposit rate control authority to nonfederally insured financial institutions,
including mutual savings banks in states where state officials lacked comparable authority and where uninsured savings
deposits exceeded 20 percent of total savings deposits. Only Massachusetts would be affected by the provision.
Increased the authorization of the Federal Home Loan Bank to borrow funds from $1 billion to $4 billion, required that
the rate charged on such borrowing be set at the current market yield on Treasury obligations, and provided the
Treasury with a positive mandate from Congress to permit such borrowing authority to be used in order to prevent
drastic reductions in housing projects. It did not remove the Treasury' veto over Home Loan Bank borrowing.
Strengthened the FRB' authority to administer the regulation establishing the maximum interest  rate commercial
banks could pay on savings and time deposits, closing a loophole permitting large banks to obtain funds in excess of the
limitations through bank-holding companies, and clarifying the FRB' authority to control buying in situations in which the
bank directly or indirectly used funds obtained by the bank' affiliates through the issuing of securities which, had they
been issued by the bank, would be deposits.
Amended the Federal Reserve Act to give the FRB the authority to establish reserve requirements of up to 22 percent
on foreign-bank borrowing, regardless of whether the borrowing was classified as a time deposit or demand deposit.
Permitted the establishment of voluntary credit controls  such as those practiced during the Korean war by repealing
a prohibition against reactivating voluntary credit  restraints in the Defense Production Act and restoring the
authority of the act.
Agency Views. Administration spokesmen supported passage of sections of the bill which extended rate ceilings to
savings institutions not covered by federal deposit insurance and buttressing Government power to regulate
commercial paper and Eurodollar trading. However, they opposed a provision which took away from the Treasury
Secretary the veto power over Home Loan Bank borrowings in the open market (the provision was deleted by the
Committee). And the FRB opposed Proxmire' “intent of Congress” provision, offered as a substitute for the 1968
measure to supplement the flow of funds to housing.
Minority Views. Wallace F. Bennett (R Utah), John G. Tower (R Texas), Edward W. Brooke (R Mass.) and Charles H.
Percy (R Ill.) opposed sections of S 2577 restoring authority used during the Korean war to establish voluntary 
credit controls . Bennett and Tower also opposed provisions permitting federal authorities to set maximum rates on
state institutions if state agencies had not been organized by July 31, 1970, thus providing rate control authority
similar to that held by the Federal Government. They also opposed including the “intent of Congress” statement
concerning Treasury-borrowing authority.
Additional Views. Charles E. Goodell (R N.Y.) opposed restoration of voluntary credit controls  as “wholly
ineffectual (since) the Administration has testified that it has no intention of using such authority.”
Floor Action
The Senate Nov. 13 passed S 2577 by a 69–14 roll-call vote without amendments and sent the measure to the
House. (See vote 134, p. 28–S.)
Prior to passing the bill, the Senate rejected on a 30–49 roll call an amendment offered by Wallace Bennett (R
Utah) which would have struck from the bill sections reactivating the authority under which the Federal Government
could administer a voluntary credit -restraint program such as the one used during the Korean War. (See vote
133, p. 28–S.)
During debate, Bennett called for rejection of part of the bill which changed federal regulation of state financial
institutions by extending federal deposit rate-control authority to nonfederally insured financial institutions, including
mutual savings banks in states where state officials lacked comparable authority and where uninsured savings
deposits exceeded 20 percent of total savings deposits.
He also opposed a section which provided unappropriated, subsidized assistance of up to $4 billion to members of
the Federal Home Loan Bank system through Treasury borrowing.
Charles H. Percy (R Ill.) also opposed the section of the bill restoring voluntary credit  control authority to the
Federal Government.
PROVISIONS—Provisions were the same as in the version of the bill reported by the Banking and Currency
Committee. (See above.)
AMENDMENT REJECTED
Oct. 13—Wallace Bennett (R Utah)—Remove provisions of S 2577 restoring to the Federal Government authority to
establish voluntary credit controls . Roll-call vote, 30–49.
House
COMMITTEE—House Banking and Currency; Wright Patman (D Texas), chairman.
ACTION—Dec. 15 reported favorably a bill (HR 15091—H Rept 91–755) to lower interest  rates, fight inflation,

ACTION—Dec. 15 reported favorably a bill (HR 15091—H Rept 91–755) to lower interest  rates, fight inflation,
and help housing, small business and employment. HR 15091, a clean bill, was introduced Dec. 4 by Patman and
19 cosponsors from the Banking and Currency Committee after hearings were held on HR 13939, the original bill
accompanying S 2577.
Major Provisions. HR 15091 as reported:
Extended until  March 22, 1971, the authority to establish flexible ceilings on rates paid by financial institutions on time
and savings deposits (Regulation Q).
Extended Federal deposit rate control authority to non-federally insured financial institutions where state officials lacked
comparable authority and where uninsured savings deposits exceeded 20 percent of total savings deposits (in
Massachusetts).
Created a secondary market for home mortgages through the Federal Home Loan Bank System.
Simplified the premium structure of an insured Federal Savings and Loan Insurance Corporation (FSLIC) and made an
additional several hundred thousand dollars available for home mortgage lending.
Authorized the FRB to establish reserve requirements up to 22 percent on funds borrowed by U.S. member banks from
foreign-owned banks.
Authorized national banks to grant mortgage loans of up to 90 percent of the appraised value of the home and to permit
such mortgages to run for 30 years. Existing law limited such mortgages to 80 percent of value and 25 years maturity.
Increased the insurance coverage on deposits in federally insured banks and shares in federally insured savings and
loan associations from $15,000 to $25,000.
Granted the President the power to determine when restrictions in terms, amount and interest  rate on all forms of 
credit  were necessary, and authorized him to instruct the Federal Reserve Board to institute selective credit
controls  in order to prevent or control inflation.
Directed the Small Business Administration to make immediately available from its business and loan investment fund
$70 million to be used for direct lending to small business investment corporations.
Two provisions in the original bill were stricken in amendments offered by Robert G. Stephens Jr. (D Ga.). The first
eliminated provisions authorizing the FRB to purchase federally guaranteed mortgages and stating the intent of
Congress that this authority be used to assist in meeting national housing goals. The second amendment eliminated
provisions which authorized purchase of up to $6 billion in federally guaranteed mortgages by the FRB.
Minority Views. In a statement of minority views, a number of members of the House Banking and Currency
Committee, including William B. Widnall (R N.J.), Florence P. Dwyer (R N.J.), W.E. Brock III (R Tenn.), Del Clawson (R
Calif.), Albert W. Johnson (R Pa.), J. William Stanton (R Ohio), Chester L. Mize (R Kan.), Ben Blackburn (R Ga.), Garry
E. Brown (R Mich.), Lawrence G. Williams (R Pa.), Chalmers P. Wylie (R Ohio), Margaret M. Heckler (R Mass.) and
William O. Cowger (R Ky.) said rather than be brought to the floor, HR 15091 should be “placed on the back burner
until and unless full and complete hearings are held on its wide-ranging freewheeling provisions.” The bill, they said,
granted extremely broad authorities to federal agencies without providing the restrictions that should be imposed to
protect the position of the Federal Government and the public.
Floor Action
The House Dec. 17 passed HR 15091 by a 260–136 roll-call vote after an unsuccessful attempt by Committee
Chairman Wright Patman (D Texas) to restore the two provisions stricken by the Banking and Currency Committee
and an attempt by William B. Widnall (R N.J.) to substitute the Senate version of the bill (S 2577) for the House
provisions. (See vote 163, 76-H.)
“Many of our Republican colleagues have been dragging their feet, using every tactic in the book to prevent a
House vote on this issue…I do not know whether the Republican obstruction has been prompted from the White
House. I hope this is not the case,” Patman commented.
Minority members of the Committee said they were told only that they would be voting on an extension of Regulation
Q authority, and had therefore not been prepared at a hearing set to study it, to discuss additional provisions
included in HR 15091.
House Rules Committee Chairman William M. Colmer (D Miss.) said that after the Banking and Currency Committee
hearing, the Rules Committee had decided to permit introduction on the House floor of a resolution (H Res 755)
which would open debate on HR 15091 but would give the House the option of passing instead H J Res 1034,
which would have extended for 60 days Regulation Q while further hearings were held on HR 15091.
Patman introduced for debate and a vote by the full House the two sections of the original House bill stricken in
Committee. The Committee amendment to strike provisions authorizing purchases of up to $6 billion in federally
guaranteed mortgages was upheld, 233–170. (See vote 161, p. 76-H.)

The Committee amendment striking provisions which would authorize the Federal Reserve Board to purchase
federally guaranteed mortgages and would state the intent of Congress that the authority be used to assist in
meeting national housing goals was adopted by the House on a 231–172 roll call. (See vote 160, p. 76-H.)
A motion by Widnall to strike all but the title of the bill and substitute for it the provisions of S 2577, the Senate

After these votes, the House by voice votes adopted an amendment substituting the language of the House-passed
bill (HR 10591) for the Senate bill and passed the amended S 2577.
AMENDMENTS ACCEPTED
Dec. 17—Committee amendment to strike provisions of original bill authorizing purchases of up to $6 billion in
federally guaranteed mortgages by the FRB. Roll call, 233–170.
Committee amendment to strike provisions authorizing the FRB to purchase federally guaranteed mortgages and
stating the intent of Congress that the authority be used to assist in meeting national housing needs. Roll call, 231–
172.
Conference Action
House and Senate conferees Dec. 18 submitted a conference report (H Rept 91–769) resolving the differences in
the House and Senate versions.
Conferees accepted the House-proposed extension of Regulation Q authority to March 22, 1971, rather than the
proposed Senate extension until Sept. 22, 1970.
A Senate provision authorizing the Federal Home Loan Bank System to operate a secondary mortgage market was
dropped from the final bill, as was a House provision which would allow national banks during the next 15 months to
make mortgage loans of up to 90 percent of appraised value and with up to 30 years maturity.
In other actions, the conferees:
Accepted the House provision for an increase in maximum insurance protection from $15,000 to $25,000, but amended
the provision to set the maximum at $20,000.
Provided both voluntary credit  control authority, as in the Senate bill,  and Presidential standby authority to instruct
the Federal Reserve Board to set credit  limits, as in the House version, to aid the President in combating inflation.
Eliminated a Senate provision stating the intent of Congress that the Secretary of the Treasury use his authority to
permit members of the Home Loan Bank System to supply funds to the mortgage market in times of tight money or
rising interest rates; but retained other sections of the provision.
Final Action
The conference report on S 2577 was adopted Dec. 19 by the House after brief debate by a 359-4 roll-call vote.
(See vote 170, p. 78-H.)
The Senate by voice vote approved the report the same day. The President signed the bill (PL 91–151) Dec. 23.
