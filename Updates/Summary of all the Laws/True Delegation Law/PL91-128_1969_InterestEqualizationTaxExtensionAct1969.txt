Equalization Tax, Ammunition
An article from CQ Almanac 1969
Congress Nov. 20 cleared for the President's signature a bill (HR 12829) extending the Interest Equalization Tax, a balance of
payments measure, until March 31, 1971. The President signed the bill Nov. 26 (PL 91 - 128).
The bill contained a controversial Senate amendment repealing the provision of the 1968 Gun Control Act requiring sellers of
shotgun and rifle ammunition to register purchasers. The amendment left in force the registration requirement for handgun
cartridges and ammunition, mainly .22 caliber rimfire, that is interchangeable between rifles and handguns.
The equalization tax, which was first enacted in 1964, applied to acquisitions of foreign securities by Americans. Its purpose was
to reduce the outflow of dollars by increasing the cost to foreigners of raising capital in the United States and reducing the
desirability of foreign securities to Americans. It did not apply to direct investment in foreign businesses.
The tax technically expired July 31, but subsequent 30-day extensions kept it in force to Sept. 30. (See box this page.) It expired
when Sen. Edward M. Kennedy (D Mass.), acting as majority floor leader, refused on Sept. 29 to call up HR 12829, which the
House had passed Aug. 7. Kennedy's action followed refusal of Sen. Wallace F. Bennett (R Utah) to withdraw his controversial
ammunition amendment to the otherwise un-controversial bill. The Gun Control Act was adopted in the wake of the assassination
of Kennedy's brother, Sen. Robert F. Kennedy (D N.Y.). (1968 Almanac p. 549)
Bennett's amendment, which had 46 cosponsors, was aimed at exempting hunting ammunition from the registration requirement.
Originally it included .22 caliber cartridges. After he agreed on Oct. 8 to limit the exemption to shotgun shells and ammunition (or
its components) suitable only for rifles generally available in commerce, the Senate leadership agreed to take up the bill. The
Senate passed it Oct. 9.
PROVISIONS—As cleared, HR 12829:
Extended the Interest Equalization Tax until  March 31, 1971.
Gave the President authority to reduce the tax on new foreign securities without reducing the tax on outstanding securities.
Granted exemption from the tax to certain financing transactions in connection with U.S. exports.
Exempted certain leases having substantially the same effect as sales in connection with U.S. exports.
Repealed the registration requirement of the 1968 Gun Control Act as it applied to shotgun and most rifle ammunition.
Made the provisions effective as of Sept. 30, 1969.
Background
The Interest Equalization Tax was introduced as a temporary weapon in the U.S. balance-of-payments struggle following an
increase in the private long-term capital outflow from $2.88 billion in 1962 to $3.67 billion in 1963, an increase of 27 percent. The
law was enacted by Congress in 1964 and made retroactive to July 17, 1963, the date it was proposed by President Kennedy.
(1964 Almanac p. 548)
Originally, the law imposed a flat 15 percent tax rate, (equivalent to 1 percent annual interest) on U.S. purchases of foreign
stocks and bonds which had a maturity of 28½ years or longer. A lower graduated rate was set for bonds with an earlier maturity.
It was intended that the tax would be passed on to the foreign sellers of the securities, either as increased interest or as a
discount from the purchase price. This was an effort to more nearly align the low U.S. interest rates with the higher rates on the

Final 1963 balance-of-payments figures indicated that the tax did hold down long-term foreign borrowing in U.S. money markets.
Sales of new foreign securities to U.S. residents fell to $251 million in the second half of 1963 from a first half total of $999 million.
If the first-half outflow had continued to the end of 1963, the estimated drain on the U.S. money market would have been $4.6
billion, an increase of 60 percent from 1962.
The 1964 Act gave the President discretionary power to apply the tax to bank loans with a maturity of one to three years. He
utilized that power in February 1965. Later that year, Congress extended the effective period of the Act to July 31, 1967, and also
broadened the Act to include taxation of other debt obligations which mature in one to three years. (1965 Almanac p. 872)
In extending the Act in 1967 to July 31, 1969, the tax rate was increased from 1 percent annual effective interest equivalent to
1.5 percent. The President also was given discretionary power to vary the tax rate from zero to 150 percent of the original 15
percent rate if he determined that adjustment was necessary to limit foreign security acquisitions to a level consistent with
balance-of-payments objectives.
In August 1967, the President made use of his power to vary the tax rate and set the rate on stocks and longterm bonds at 18.75
percent or an annual interest equivalent of 1.25 percent. As U.S. interest rates rose to more nearly the level of interest rates
abroad, this provision was used a second time. On April 4, 1969, the President lowered the tax rate to 11.25 percent or 0.75
percent equivalent annual interest. (For further background, see 1967 Almanac p. 255; for other 1969 Administration action, see
story on foreign investment controls.)
House
COMMITTEE—House Ways and Means; Wilbur D. Mills (D Ark.), chairman.
ACTION—July 21 reported by unanimous vote HR 12829 (H Rept 91-383), a bill unchanged from the Administration request
except that it extended the Interest Equalization Tax for 20 months rather than 18 months as requested by the President.
Major Provisions. As reported by the Committee, HR 12829:
Extended the Interest Equalization Tax for 20 months until  March 31, 1971.
Gave the President authority to reduce the tax rate on new security issues. Existing law provided means for the President to vary the
total tax rate only on foreign securities. Under HR 12829, the President would be permitted to lower the rate on new issues, but hold
the rate on outstanding issues.
Committee Views. In reporting HR 12829, the Committee noted that while the Interest Equalization Tax had succeeded in
moderating the outflow of private capital, it had not succeeded in eliminating it. The Committee also acknowledged progress in
eliminating the deficit in the U.S. balance of payments. The Committee cautioned, however, that without the interest equalization
tax progress made in moderating capital outflow and other balance-of-payments programs would be jeopardized.
Another consideration, according to the report, was the precarious state of the U.S. balance of payments as the United States
was attempting to implement special drawing rights on the International Monetary Fund. The U.S. balance-of-payments position
deteriorated during the first quarter of 1969 from a 1968 surplus of $93 million to 1969 first-quarter deficit of $1.7 billion.
Floor Action
The House Aug. 7 by voice vote passed and sent to the Senate HR 12829 extending the interest equalization tax until March 31,
1971. No changes were made in the bill on the floor.
Senate
COMMITTEE—Senate Finance; Russell B. Long (D La.), chairman.
HELD HEARINGS—Sept. 3 on HR 12829, a bill to extend the interest equalization tax for 20 months and give the President the
power to vary the tax rate on new foreign security issues sold in the United States.
TESTIMONY—Sept. 3—Paul A. Volcker, Under Secretary of the Treasury for Monetary Affairs, speaking for the Administration,
urged extension of the tax as an interim measure for balance-of-payments protection. He said the tax measure was necessary
because of a continuing depreciation in the trade balance. He noted, however, that the President was committed to “relaxing and
dismantling” selective controls on capital exports as the economic situation permitted.
Volcker termed extension of the interest equalization tax “the minimum insurance necessary to guard against the risk of
potentially large portfolio outflows.”
He cited lower interest rates for bonds in the United States than in other money markets as evidence of need for continuing the
tax. He said that countries exempt from the tax had shown continued interest in borrowing in the United States. He also said that
the tax aided in reducing the balance-of-payments problem by causing U.S.-owned companies overseas to look for investment
capital abroad.

Sen. Jacob K. Javits (R N.Y.), an opponent of the tax since it was created in 1963, “reluctantly” urged extension of the tax
“because there is nothing to take its place.” Javits, however, said that the tax should be extended only for one year and that a
$100 million exemption for Japan should be repealed.
He called the tax “a protective tariff on imported securities” and said that the Administration should use the year extension to
phase out the tax. Noting the U.S. balance-of-payments position, he said that removal of the tax now “would cast the United
States in a poor light in the international financial community.”
Javits said that repeal of the little-used Japanese exemption would have no ill effects on Japanese-American relations. He
argued that the Japanese economy had outgrown the need for favored treatment.
ACTION—Sept. 24 reported HR 12829 (S Rept 91-428) to extend the interest equalization tax until March 31, 1971.
The Committee added technical amendments and an unrelated amendment which would repeal some recordkeeping
requirements of the Gun Control Act of 1968 (PL 90-618). The gun control amendment, which was sponsored by Sen. Wallace F.
Bennett (R Utah), would repeal sections of the Internal Revenue Code which require keeping records on sales of sporting
ammunition, including 22 caliber ammunition and rounds for shotguns and rifles. (For related developments, see story on gun,
ammunition control.)
Major Provisions. As amended by the Senate Finance Committee, HR 12829:
Extended the interest equalization tax until  March 31, 1971.
Gave the President authority to reduce the tax on new foreign security issues without reducing the rate on outstanding foreign
securities.
Made numerous technical changes in the existing interest equalization tax law, including extending the tax to investments in foreign
leases which were made primarily as a financing transaction.
Repealed provisions of the Gun Control Act of 1968 requiring sellers of shotgun and rifle ammunition to register purchasers.
Floor Action
The Senate Oct. 9 by voice vote passed an amended bill (HR 12829) extending the interest equalization tax and repealing a
provision of the Gun Control Act of 1968 requiring merchants to keep certain records on sales of shotgun and rifle ammunition.
The provision dealing with ammunition sales had been adopted by the Finance Committee after it was offered by Wallace F.
Bennett (R Utah). As adopted by the Committee, the amendment also exempted 22-caliber rimfire ammunition from the record-
keeping requirement of the Gun Control Act.
Edward M. Kennedy (D Mass.), as Acting Majority Leader, Sept. 29 refused to call the bill up for floor action because of the
Bennett amendment. Kennedy said Senators who opposed the gun control amendment deserved time to prepare their arguments.
The delay resulted in the expiration of the existing interest equalization tax Sept. 30. The tax had expired July 31 but was
extended until Sept. 30 by temporary one-month extensions. (See box, p. 334.)
Bennett Oct. 8 modified the Committee amendment under the Senate's unanimous consent rule to exclude .22-caliber
ammunition.
The Senate Oct. 9 adopted the modified Committee amendment repealing the record-keeping requirement by a 65-16 roll-call
vote. (See vote 100, p. 22-S.)
Before the vote on the amendment, Thomas J. Dodd (D Conn.) moved to table the amendment and thus kill it. His motion was
rejected 19-65, on a roll-call vote. (See vote 99, p. 22-S.)
Also before the amendment was adopted, the Senate modified it further by accepting by voice vote an amendment by Bennett to
specify that the rifle ammunition bookkeeping exemption applied to ammunition for “rifles generally available on the commercial
market.”
PROVISIONS—As passed by the Senate, HR 12829:
Repealed provisions, except for .22-caliber ammunition, of the Gun Control Act of 1968 requiring record-keeping of sales of
ammunition for rifles and shotguns.
Extended the interest equalization tax on foreign securities purchased by Americans until  March 31, 1971.
Gave the President authority to reduce the tax on new foreign security issues without reducing the tax on outstanding foreign
securities.
Extended the law to tax investments in foreign leases which were made primarily as a financing transaction.

The House Nov. 17 by a roll-call vote of 313-36 agreed to a conference with the Senate on HR 12829.(See vote 130, p. 64-H.)
A roll-call vote was made necessary after objection was heard to a unanimous-consent request to send the bill to conference.
Subsequently, supporters of the bill obtained a rule from the Rules Committee to send the bill to conference. The vote was on
adoption of the rule.
REPORT. House-Senate conferees Nov. 18 filed a report (H Rept 91-656) on HR 12829. House conferees receded on all Senate
amendments to the bill. The major House concession was on the ammunition amendment, which was not germane to the House
bill. Conferees agreed to a minor clarifying amendment concerning the effective date. Dates differed in the Senate and House
versions because temporary 30-day extensions of the tax were voted after the House approved the bill but before the Senate
voted on it. The bill was made retroactive to Sept. 30.
ACTION. The House Nov. 19 by a 334-47 roll-call vote adopted the conference report. (See vote 134, p. 66-H.) The Senate by
voice vote adopted the conference report Nov. 20.

Temporary Extensions
Two temporary, 30-day extensions to the interest equalization tax were cleared by Congress pending final passage of HR 12829.
The existing law (PL 90-59) was scheduled to expire July 31, 1969.
HR 13079—To continue interest equalization tax to Aug. 31, 1969.
Reported from House Ways and Means Committee (H Rept 91-392) July 24.
Passed House July 28 by voice vote.
Passed Senate July 31 by voice vote.
Signed into law Aug. 2, 1969 (PL 91-50).
HR 10107—To continue the interest equalization tax to Sept. 30, 1969.
Reported from House Ways and Means Committee (H Rept 91-162) April 23.
Passed House May 13 by voice vote.
Reported from Senate Finance Committee (S Rept 91-373) Aug. 11.
Passed Senate amended Aug. 11 by voice vote.
House agreed to Senate amendments Aug. 12 by voice vote.
Signed into law Aug. 25, 1969 (PL 91-65).
